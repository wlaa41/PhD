Simulation-trained flight, deployed to real hardware without retraining.

This is a working literature review you can read and learn from, not a folder of notes. It surveys how robot control policies trained entirely in simulation are deployed zero-shot on real drones, extracts every method that makes those deployments work, counts how often the field relies on each one, and uses that frequency evidence to locate the gap a new control model can fill.

The reading list · 5 of ~50

Each paper below has a full annotated page: the problem, the pipeline from simulator to airframe as a figure, the mathematics behind the key ideas, results, stated limitations, and what it means for this project's hardware. Extractions are from the papers' arXiv pages (first pass); every page carries its provenance and verification status honestly.

  1. 01

    FalconGym: photorealistic simulation for zero-shot vision-based flight

    Miao, Shen & Mitra · UIUC · IROS 2025 zero-shot score 95

    Builds the flight space as a Neural Radiance Field, trains a visual policy on unlimited synthetic images, and flies it on a real quadrotor with no fine-tuning: 95.8% gate-crossing success over 30 live flights with roughly 10 cm error.

    Read the annotated paper →
  2. 02

    RAPTOR: one tiny foundation policy for any quadrotor

    Eschmann, Albani & Loianno · NYU ARPL · arXiv 2026 zero-shot score 93

    A single recurrent policy with 2,084 parameters flies ten physically different real quadrotors, from 32 g to 2.4 kg, adapting in-context within milliseconds. Trained by distilling 1,000 simulated specialist teachers into one student.

    Read the annotated paper →
  3. 03

    MonoRace: winning champion-level drone racing with one camera

    Bahnam et al. · TU Delft MAVLab · arXiv 2026 zero-shot score 93 A2RL win verified

    Won the 2025 A2RL championship in Abu Dhabi against every AI team and three human world champions, at up to 100 km/h, using only a monocular rolling-shutter camera and an IMU, with motor commands computed onboard at 500 Hz.

    Read the annotated paper →
  4. 04

    E2E-Fly: an integrated training-to-deployment system

    Sun et al. · SJTU · arXiv 2026 zero-shot score 93

    One stack from a differentiable-physics simulator through system identification, domain randomization and latency compensation to a two-stage validation ladder and six real-hardware tasks. The closest published blueprint to this project's own implementation plan.

    Read the annotated paper →
  5. 05

    Learning to Fly in Seconds

    Eschmann, Albani & Loianno · NYU ARPL · RA-L 2024 zero-shot score 92 designated baseline

    An asymmetric actor-critic policy for direct motor control, trained in 18 seconds on a laptop thanks to a simulator that runs five months of flight per wall-clock second, then deployed on a Crazyflie's microcontroller. Open source, and the designated reproduction baseline for this project.

    Read the annotated paper →

The programme · how this review is produced

Everything here is generated by a two-track pipeline with human decision gates, specified in full in the repository's docs. The literature track discovers and triages ~120 candidates, extracts the top ~50 in weekly batches of ten, verifies every extraction against a second independent reader (NotebookLM) before it is trusted, and clusters the papers' stated limitations into the gap analysis. The implementation track runs in parallel from week 1: infrastructure bring-up, reproduction of a published baseline through to a tethered flight, then a pilot experiment on the selected gap, so the transfer report contains real hardware evidence rather than only a survey.

Three gates are never delegated: corpus lock (Gate 1), direction and pilot approval (Gate 2), and report sign-off after a clean citation audit (Gate 3). Every claim on this site resolves to a source in under a minute: sentence → paper id → registry entry → PDF page.
