E2E-Fly: An Integrated Training-to-Deployment System for End-to-End Quadrotor Autonomy

Fangyu Sun, Fanxing Li, Linzuo Zhang, Yu Hu, Renbiao Jin, Shuyu Wu, Wenxian Yu & Danping Zou

TL;DR The paper's answer to sim-to-real flight is a whole system, not a technique: one integrated stack running from a high-performance simulator — supporting both differentiable-physics learning and reinforcement learning, with structured rewards per task — through system identification, sensor-noise modelling, domain randomization and latency compensation, into a two-stage validation gate (sim-to-sim transfer, then hardware-in-the-loop), and out onto real vehicles: six end-to-end control tasks deployed on two physical quadrotor platforms. The authors claim it is the first work to systematically unify differentiable physical learning with training, validation and real-world deployment.

1 · The problem

The gap this paper attacks is not a missing technique but the seams between existing ones. Rendering, physical modelling, sensing and latency each leak a little reality on the way from simulator to vehicle — and the leaks compound.

Each seam has a known symptom. Visual rendering is too slow to feed policy training at scale; physical models mis-state mass, drag and actuator behaviour; sensors on the real vehicle carry noise and bias the simulator never emitted; and the perception–action loop on hardware runs with delays the training loop did not have. Point tools exist for every one of these — system identification, domain randomization, latency compensation, noise models — but, the authors argue, no unified platform stitches them into a single training-to-deployment workflow, and none integrates differentiable physics learning into end-to-end training. The consequence they emphasise is reproducibility: recent work demonstrates individual end-to-end tasks, yet few systems offer a systematic zero-shot transfer pipeline that another laboratory could simply run.

2 · The approach at a glance

Read top to bottom, the system is a sequence of leak-plugging stages, each aimed at one of the seams from §1: the simulator plugs the rendering-and-training seam, alignment plugs the modelling and sensing seams, the robustness stage hedges whatever alignment missed, and the two validation stages exist to catch anything that still slipped through — before it costs an airframe.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Differentiable physics learning, with RL as the hedge

The training framework's distinctive ingredient is a simulator whose dynamics are differentiable, so that policy learning can use analytic gradients through the physics rather than sampled estimates:

$$ s_{t+1} = f_\psi(s_t,\, a_t), \qquad \nabla_{\theta}\, J \;=\; \sum_{t} \frac{\partial J}{\partial s_t}\, \frac{\partial s_t}{\partial \theta} $$
Notation ours (canonical form of backpropagation through a differentiable simulator): \(f_\psi\) the simulated dynamics with physical parameters \(\psi\); the policy parameters \(\theta\) enter through the actions \(a_t\), so \(\partial s_t / \partial \theta\) unrolls through the dynamics. The paper's exact formulation awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · first-order versus zeroth-order learning signals

Model-free RL estimates the policy gradient from returns alone — a zeroth-order signal whose variance is paid for in samples. A differentiable simulator supplies first-order gradients directly, which can be dramatically more sample-efficient. The catch is that those gradients are only as good as the computation graph: through contacts, discontinuities or long horizons they can be biased or exploding, and a first-order optimiser will happily follow a wrong gradient. Supporting both paradigms in one simulator is therefore the pragmatic hedge, not an indulgence: use analytic gradients where the physics is smooth, and fall back to model-free RL where it is not — same environments, same rewards, same deployment path.

3.2 Real-to-sim alignment: identify the model, model the sensors

Before any policy transfers, the simulator is pulled towards the specific vehicle. System identification fits the simulator's physical parameters to logged real flight data:

$$ \psi^{*} \;=\; \arg\min_{\psi}\; \sum_{t} \big\lVert\, s^{\mathrm{real}}_{t+1} \;-\; f_\psi\big(s^{\mathrm{real}}_{t},\, a^{\mathrm{real}}_{t}\big) \big\rVert^{2} $$
Notation ours — one-step prediction error over real state–action logs; the paper's identification procedure and parameter set await the full-PDF pass.

Symmetrically, the observation channel is corrupted the way the real sensors corrupt it, with the noise fitted empirically rather than assumed Gaussian by fiat:

$$ z_t \;=\; h(s_t) + \varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \text{empirical noise model} $$
Notation ours: \(h\) the sensor map, \(\varepsilon_t\) drawn from a model fitted to real sensor logs. The paper's noise families and fitting method await the full-PDF pass.

The two fits address different seams: identification makes the simulated vehicle behave like the real one; noise modelling makes the simulated observations look like the real ones. A policy trained on clean simulated sensing meets, at deployment, an input distribution it has never seen — alignment closes that gap on both sides of the state.

3.3 Robustness: randomize what identification missed, compensate the clock

Identification is never exact, so the residual uncertainty is randomized over during training — the policy is asked to do well in expectation across a distribution of plausible worlds:

$$ \max_{\theta}\;\; \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim p(\xi)} \big[\, J(\theta;\, \xi) \,\big] $$
Notation ours: \(\xi\) the randomized simulation parameters (dynamics, delays, disturbances), \(p(\xi)\) centred on the identified model. The paper's randomization ranges await the full-PDF pass.

Latency is handled explicitly rather than randomized away: the policy acts on an observation that is honest about being \(d\) steps old, augmented with the actions issued in the interim:

$$ o_t \;=\; \big(\, z_{t-d},\; a_{t-d},\, \ldots,\, a_{t-1} \,\big) $$
Notation ours: \(z_{t-d}\) the most recent sensing to have arrived, followed by the action history since it was captured. The paper's compensation scheme awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why the action history restores what latency removes

With \(d\) steps of sensing-to-actuation delay, the vehicle the policy is steering is not the vehicle it is looking at: by the time an action lands, the state has evolved for \(d\) further steps. The policy must therefore implicitly predict the present from stale evidence. The missing information is recoverable, because the controller itself caused most of the intervening evolution — it knows every action it issued since \(z_{t-d}\) was captured. Appending \(a_{t-d}, \ldots, a_{t-1}\) to the observation hands the network exactly the inputs a model-based predictor would integrate forward, and lets it learn the roll-out implicitly. Trained this way in simulation, the policy meets real latency as a familiar condition rather than a distribution shift.

3.4 Two-stage validation: sim-to-sim, then hardware-in-the-loop

Between training and flight sit two deliberately cheap gates. First, the policy is transferred sim-to-sim — run in a second simulator it was not trained in — which exposes overfitting to the training simulator's particular integrator, contact model or timing quirks while a crash still costs nothing. Second, hardware-in-the-loop testing runs the policy through the real flight computer and control interface with the vehicle on the bench, which catches integration faults — units, frames, timing, quantisation, the dedicated low-level interface itself — that no simulator run can surface. Neither stage proves the policy will fly; each removes a class of failure whose discovery in the air would cost an airframe, and both together are far cheaper than one crash.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (verified at S5, 10 Jul 2026, from the full text): designing "effective and smoothly differentiable reward functions" for long-horizon tasks is hard; back-propagation through long sequences suffers "vanishing or exploding gradients"; gradient-based optimization is prone to "convergence toward suboptimal behaviors"; BPTT is "highly sensitive" to sparse rewards; and it "requires constructing a fully differentiable gradient chain", not always feasible. The authors' stated future direction: combine RL (high-level exploration) with differentiable simulation (precise refinement).
  • Verified detail the abstract lacked: the two platforms are VIS-R (Radax X4 onboard computer, Intel N100, RealSense D435i stereo camera) and VIS-H (hardware-in-the-loop, FPV camera to a ground station), both on Betaflight STM32F7 flight controllers; BPTT converges in ~1,200 s, "less than 30% of PPO's training time"; vision-based racing achieved a "100% success rate across three racetracks"; the six tasks are hovering, tracking, landing, racing, visual landing, and racing in cluttered environments.
  • Ours: how much real flight data the system-identification stage needs remains the practical question for a new airframe.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Of everything in the corpus, E2E-Fly is the closest published blueprint to this project's implementation track: its validation ladder is this project's flight ladder with different names, arrived at independently by a 2026 Chinese-institution group. That convergence is itself evidence the staging is sound — and it makes the full PDF unusually actionable: reading it early pressure-tests the I1–I3 plan against a group that has already run the same pipeline to real flight, before this project commits hardware to it. Its framing of sim-to-real as a problem of seams rather than techniques also supports the project's integration-first pipeline design: the decision to build the stages together and validate the joints, rather than perfect any single stage in isolation.
