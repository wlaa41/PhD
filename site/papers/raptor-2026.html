RAPTOR: A Foundation Policy for Quadrotor Control

Jonas Eschmann, Dario Albani & Giuseppe Loianno

TL;DR A single neural network with 2,084 parameters — three layers, small enough to print on a page — controls ten physically different real quadrotors, from a 32 g toy to a 2.4 kg platform, with no per-platform tuning, system identification or retraining. The recipe: sample 1,000 simulated quadrotor variants, train a specialist RL teacher for each, then distil all of them into one tiny recurrent student whose hidden state figures out, within milliseconds of flight, which kind of vehicle it is sitting on.

1 · The problem

Learned flight controllers are bespoke. A policy trained for one quadrotor's mass, motors and propellers usually fails on the next airframe, and the standard fix — re-identify, re-train, re-deploy — is exactly the engineering burden learning was meant to remove.

The authors frame this as the foundation-model question for low-level control: language models generalize across tasks because they were trained across a distribution of tasks; can a flight controller generalize across vehicles because it was trained across a distribution of vehicles? The obstacle is that the relationship between commands and motion differs per platform, so a single reactive policy cannot work — something has to identify the platform, implicitly or explicitly, at deployment time.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 A distribution over vehicles, not a vehicle

Rather than model one quadrotor well, RAPTOR samples a population of them. Each variant \(\xi_i\) draws mass, inertia, motor time constants, thrust maps, frame geometry and propeller characteristics from a wide distribution \(p(\xi)\). Where classic domain randomization perturbs one nominal platform to make a policy robust, here the distribution is the training target — the student is asked to fly all of them, which forces it to learn platform inference rather than a fixed controller.

3.2 One specialist teacher per variant

For each sampled vehicle \(\xi_i\), an RL teacher \(\pi^*_i\) is trained to (near-)optimality with full access to that variant's simulator. Teachers are cheap in simulation and never need to transfer, so they can exploit privileged state freely.

Concept · the RL objective

Each teacher maximizes its variant's expected discounted return $$ J(\pi) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi,\, \xi_i} \Big[ \textstyle\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^{t}\, r(s_t, a_t) \Big], $$ with reward \(r\) shaped for stable, accurate flight on that specific platform. Nothing here is transferable yet — the transfer trick is in the next step.

3.3 Meta-imitation: distilling 1,000 teachers into one student

The proposed Meta-Imitation Learning algorithm trains a single student \(\pi_\phi\) to match whichever teacher governs the platform it is currently flying, using only observations the real vehicle will have:

$$ \min_{\phi}\;\; \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim p(\xi)}\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\phi \,|\, \xi} \Big[ \sum_t \, D\!\big( \pi^*_{\xi}(\cdot \mid s_t)\,\big\|\; \pi_\phi(\cdot \mid o_t, h_t) \big) \Big] $$
Notation ours (reconstructed from the abstract's description): \(D\) a policy divergence, \(s_t\) the teacher's privileged state, \(o_t\) the deployable observation, \(h_t\) the student's recurrent state. The paper's exact formulation awaits the full-PDF pass.

Two details matter. The expectation over trajectories is taken under the student's own behaviour, DAgger-style, so it learns to correct its own mistakes rather than only the teacher's states. And the student never sees the variant parameters \(\xi\) — it must infer them from how the vehicle responds.

3.4 In-context adaptation through recurrence

The inference mechanism is the recurrent state. At each control step the student folds the latest observation and its own previous action into a hidden state:

$$ h_t = f_\phi(h_{t-1},\, o_t,\, a_{t-1}), \qquad a_t \sim \pi_\phi(\cdot \mid o_t,\, h_t). $$
The pair (command issued, motion observed) is exactly the evidence a system identifier would use; \(h_t\) becomes an implicit, continuously-updated estimate of the platform.
Concept · why recurrence performs system identification

If the same action produces different accelerations on two airframes, a memoryless policy cannot tell them apart, but a recurrent one can compare "what I commanded" with "what happened" across a few steps and condition its behaviour on the discrepancy. This is the same idea as RMA's latent adaptation module, taken to its minimal form: no separate adaptation network, just the hidden state of a tiny RNN. The authors report adaptation "within milliseconds" — a handful of 100 Hz-scale control steps.

3.5 Why so small: 2,084 parameters

The student has three layers and 2,084 parameters — orders of magnitude below typical policy networks. The authors argue minimality reduces overfitting to the training distribution; it also makes deployment trivial: this fits comfortably on any flight controller, with no accelerator, quantization or pruning.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (verified at S5, 10 Jul 2026, from the full text): a significant part of the remaining sim-to-real gap comes from flight-controller and infrastructure delays; the policy relies heavily on linear-velocity observations for its implicit system identification; the domain randomization range shows limits on out-of-distribution platforms (e.g. very high thrust-to-weight); and tracking lacks trajectory lookahead.
  • Verified detail the abstract lacked: figure-eight tracking RMSE 0.07–0.19 m (mean 0.11 m); 5.5-s trajectory RMSE 0.17–0.29 m (mean 0.20 m); 10× context-window extrapolation (50 s stable flight from 5-s training sequences); the student is a GRU trained in the L2F (Learning-to-Fly) simulator under a "very broad distribution over dynamics parameters" with ancestral sampling of physically plausible platforms.
  • Our open questions (ours): envelope aggressiveness before 2,084 parameters saturate; payload changes mid-flight; behaviour far outside \(p(\xi)\)'s support.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

RAPTOR is the foundation-policy trend at its most extreme, and it reframes the cross-platform ambition of this project: the drone-to-ground-robot extension could be a distribution design problem rather than a transfer-method problem. Practically, 2,084 parameters would run on this project's Pixhawk 6X directly — the Jetson Orin Nano becomes headroom for perception rather than a necessity for control. The meta-imitation recipe (specialist teachers → tiny recurrent student) is a direct candidate for the pilot experiment, and its in-context adaptation is the strongest current answer to the registry's rma-latent-adaptation lineage.
