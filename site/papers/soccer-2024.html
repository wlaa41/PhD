Learning agile soccer skills for a bipedal robot

Tuomas Haarnoja, Ben Moran, Guy Lever, Sandy H. Huang, Dhruva Tirumala & 23 further co-authors

TL;DR A 28-author team (DeepMind, per the corpus manifest) trained a low-cost Robotis OP3 miniature humanoid — 51 cm, 3.5 kg, 20 servos — to play simplified 1v1 soccer with deep RL. A two-stage pipeline first learns separate get-up and goal-scoring skills with Distributional MPO, then distils them into one agent trained via self-play. Targeted dynamics randomisation, perturbations, noise injection, 40 Hz control and an exponential action filter carried the policy zero-shot onto the real robot, running CPU-only on an embedded i3 NUC. Against scripted baseline controllers it walked 181% faster, turned 302% faster, got up in 63% less time and kicked at a mean 2.8 m/s.

1 · The problem

Soccer is a stress test for whole-body control: a single agent must walk, turn, kick, block, fall and get up again, and chain those skills fluidly against an adversary. Hand-scripting each behaviour on a cheap hobby-grade humanoid produces motions that are slow and conservative — and gives no principled way to compose them.

The harder question is whether deep RL can deliver such agile, contact-rich, multi-skill behaviour on real low-cost hardware at all. The platform here is deliberately modest: twenty Robotis Dynamixel XM430-350-R servomotors, no GPU or accelerator on board, and real dynamics a simulator only approximates. The paper's answer is a training recipe in which the entire behaviour is learned in simulation and then, in the authors' words, "transferred to real robots zero-shot" — no fine-tuning on real-world data anywhere in the loop.

2 · The approach at a glance

Two stages in simulation, one crossing to reality. Stage 1 trains narrow specialist skills; stage 2 merges them into a single 1v1 player and hardens it through self-play; a bundle of robustness measures applied throughout training makes the final policy survive the jump to the physical robot.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 The task and the simulator

The environment is a simplified 1v1 soccer match built on the MuJoCo physics engine and the DeepMind Control Suite, with a simulated model of the Robotis OP3 humanoid on a soccer pitch. The agent acts at 40 Hz. Observations split into two streams: proprioception — joint positions, linear acceleration, angular velocity, gravity direction and the state of the exponential action filter — and game state — the agent's velocity, ball location and velocity, opponent location and velocity, and the locations of the two goals. On the real robot the first stream comes from onboard encoders and the IMU; the second is supplied by external motion capture (§3.6).

3.2 The learner: Distributional MPO

Stage one trains the skill policies with Distributional MPO (DMPO), an off-policy actor-critic RL algorithm, combined with a distributional critic — the authors call the overall algorithm Distributional MPO, or DMPO. The policy is a deep feed-forward network outputting the mean and diagonal covariance of a multivariate Gaussian, and it maximises the expected discounted return:

$$ \mathcal{J}(\pi) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim \mu_\pi}\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^{t}\, r(\boldsymbol{s}_t)\right] $$
From the paper's Policy Optimization / Materials and Methods text: the return under the trajectory distribution induced by the policy, with discount \(\gamma\) and state reward \(r(\boldsymbol{s}_t)\).
Concept · off-policy actor-critic with a distributional critic (ours)

An off-policy learner can reuse experience gathered by older versions of the policy, which makes it sample-efficient — valuable when each simulated match is expensive to generate. The distributional part means the critic predicts a distribution over returns rather than a single expected value, giving the actor a richer learning signal. Neither ingredient is soccer-specific: the design choice is a general-purpose, data-efficient RL engine the rest of the pipeline leans on twice — once per stage.

3.3 Two stages: learn skills, then distil and self-play

The pipeline has two stages. In the first, separate skill policies are trained for scoring goals and for getting up from the ground. In the second, "the skills are distilled into a single 1v1 agent, and the agent is trained via self-play"; distillation stops once the agent surpasses a preset performance threshold, after which self-play carries on alone.

Concept · what self-play buys (ours)

A fixed scripted opponent teaches a fixed lesson. In self-play the opponent is a copy (or earlier snapshot) of the agent itself, so the curriculum sharpens automatically: every weakness the agent develops becomes something its opponent exploits, and defending against it becomes the next lesson. The cost — which the authors themselves flag (§5) — is that a curriculum generated by a moving target can make learning unstable.

3.4 The reward

The reward is a weighted sum of \(K\) shaped components:

$$ r(\boldsymbol{s}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k\, \hat{r}_k(\boldsymbol{s}) $$
From the paper's Materials and Methods. The authors state plainly that several of these auxiliary terms are hand-tuned, and count the reliance on domain-specific reward design among the method's limitations.

3.5 Crossing the gap: the transfer toolbox

No real-world data is used anywhere. Instead, transfer rests on a bundle of measures applied during simulated training — in the authors' summary, "a combination of sufficiently high-frequency control, targeted dynamics randomization, and perturbations during training in simulation enabled good-quality transfer", with the Discussion adding that "with appropriate regularization, domain randomization, and noise injected during training, safe sim-to-real transfer is possible". The policy's raw output is also smoothed before it reaches the motors, through an exponential action filter that removes high-frequency components:

$$ \boldsymbol{u}_t = 0.8\,\boldsymbol{u}_{t-1} + 0.2\,\bar{\boldsymbol{u}}_t $$
From the paper's Environment section: \(\boldsymbol{u}_t\) is the filtered control applied to the robot, \(\bar{\boldsymbol{u}}_t\) the policy's raw output. The filter state is part of the observation, so the policy knows what the motors were actually told.
Concept · why smoothing and rate matter for transfer (ours)

High-frequency action chatter is where simulators lie most: an idealised motor model tracks a jittery command faithfully, a small hobby-class servo does not. Low-pass filtering the actions keeps the policy inside the frequency band where simulation and reality agree, while running control at 40 Hz keeps the loop fast enough to catch disturbances. Feeding the filter state back as an observation makes the filtered plant Markovian again from the policy's point of view — the same trick, in miniature, that latency-compensation schemes use.

3.6 What the robot actually runs on

Deployment is deliberately spartan. The control software runs on an embedded Intel Core i3 dual-core NUC under Linux; the robot "lacks GPUs or other dedicated accelerators, so all neural network computations were run on the CPU". Each actuator's magnetic rotary encoder supplies joint positions and the IMU supplies angular velocity and linear acceleration, but the robot does not perceive the game itself: robot and ball poses come from an external OptiTrack motion-capture system (Motive 2, 14 PrimeX 22 cameras on a truss around the pitch). One honest deployment detail stands out: "the nominal control time step was 25 ms, but in practice the agent often failed to produce an action within that time" — the deployed policy had to tolerate its own missed deadlines.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026) — method: the learning pipeline relies on domain-specific knowledge and on domain randomisation; that knowledge enters through reward-function design and through training the get-up skill; no real-world data is leveraged for transfer (the approach relies solely on sim-to-real); several auxiliary reward terms are hand-tuned; and the self-play setup sometimes resulted in unstable learning.
  • Stated by the authors (same source) — hardware and deployment: the method was applied to a small robot and did not consider additional challenges (e.g. of larger robots); tracking the ball with motion capture was particularly challenging; robot performance degraded quickly over time, mainly because the hip joints became loose; the control stack was not optimised for speed (contributing to missed control deadlines); and the servo motors were not modelled in simulation.
  • Stated future work (same source): training teams of two or more agents for cooperative multi-agent soccer; and learning from on-board sensors only, with vision-based agents inferring information from a limited history of high-dimensional egocentric observations.
  • Our open questions (ours): how much of the transfer recipe survives once the OptiTrack crutch is removed and game state must come from onboard perception; whether the 40 Hz + action-filter combination generalises to platforms with far faster dynamics; and how sensitive the zero-shot result is to the unmodelled servo dynamics the authors flag — would a different actuator batch break it?

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Platform mismatch first: this is a bipedal humanoid, not a UAV, and it leans on an external OptiTrack rig to supply every game-state observation — nothing here maps directly onto an X500 V2 quad flying on an Orin Nano with onboard sensing. What earns its place in the corpus is the transfer recipe: an off-policy actor-critic (DMPO) trained entirely in MuJoCo, hardened by targeted dynamics randomisation, external perturbations, observation noise and high-rate control, with an exponential action filter smoothing the sim-to-real seam — then deployed zero-shot on CPU-only hardware. The skill-then-distil-via-self-play pipeline is a reusable pattern for composing behaviours a single reward struggles to express. And the paper's own stated gap — learning from on-board sensors only, with vision — is precisely the harder problem a zero-shot vision-based UAV transfer has to solve, which makes this a useful "before" picture of the field.
