Demonstrating Agile Flight from Pixels without State Estimation

Ismail Geles, Leonard Bauersfeld, Angel Romero, Jiaxu Xing & Davide Scaramuzza

TL;DR A quadrotor flies through gate sequences at up to 40 km/h with accelerations up to 2g, mapping the onboard camera feed directly to control commands — with no state estimator anywhere in the loop. Like a human FPV pilot, the system sees a first-person video stream and outputs the commands humans use: collective thrust and body rates. The authors present it as the first vision-based quadrotor system to navigate gate sequences at high speed while directly mapping pixels to control commands, running on "standard, off-the-shelf hardware".

1 · The problem

Every system annotated so far in this corpus puts an explicit state estimate between sensing and control: perception feeds an estimator, the estimator feeds the policy. That middle stage is also the fragile one — and this paper asks what happens if you simply remove it.

The abstract states the tension plainly: despite advances in learning-based control and computer vision, "autonomous drones still rely on explicit state estimation", while human pilots fly the same platforms to their limits from nothing but the first-person-view video stream. MonoRace (03 in this corpus) is the sharpest illustration of the conventional answer: it keeps a monocular state estimator and wins a world championship by painstakingly engineering around that estimator's failure modes, down to modelling rolling-shutter distortion at 100 km/h. Geles et al. take the opposite route — if the estimator is the stage you must engineer around, delete the stage. The question is whether reinforcement learning can close the loop from pixels to commands without the crutch of an explicit state.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Asymmetric actor–critic: privileged critic, deployable actor

Training uses reinforcement learning, and the abstract names the enabling trick: "an asymmetric actor-critic with access to privileged information". The critic — which exists only during training and never flies — is allowed to see the simulator's ground truth. The actor is restricted to observations that will actually be available on the vehicle:

$$ V_\psi(s_t) \;\;\text{(critic: privileged simulator state)}, \qquad a_t \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid o_t) \;\;\text{(actor: deployable observation)} $$
Notation ours: \(s_t\) the full simulator state available to the critic during training only, \(o_t\) the actor's abstracted observation built around the gate inner edges. The paper's exact observation vector and training algorithm await the full-PDF pass.

The asymmetry lets the value estimate stay accurate and low-variance even though the actor's view of the world is deliberately impoverished. The same device underpins Learning to Fly in Seconds (05 in this corpus), where the critic sees privileged state while the actor sees only what the Crazyflie will — here it is pushed further, because the actor's observation is not even a state, just task-relevant geometry.

3.2 The key idea: training-time sensor abstraction

Image-based RL is normally crushed by its own rendering bill: millions of training steps, each needing a rendered frame, and then a pixel-level sim-to-real gap on top. The abstract's answer: "we use the inner edges of the gates as a sensor abstraction. This simple yet robust, task-relevant representation can be simulated during training without rendering images." The policy is trained against that cheap geometric observation; only at deployment is a "Swin-transformer-based gate detector" swapped in to produce the same observation from the real camera feed.

Concept · why abstract the sensor at training time

The abstraction decouples two problems that image-based RL usually forces you to solve at once. First, rendering cost: gate edges can be projected analytically in microseconds, so RL runs at simulator speed with no renderer in the loop. Second, the pixel-level sim-to-real gap: the policy never sees a pixel, so simulated images never need to look real — the entire visual gap is absorbed by the detector, a supervised-learning component that can be trained and validated on real images independently of the policy. FalconGym (01) makes the opposite bet on the same problem: make rendering photorealistic so pixels transfer. Geles et al. make rendering unnecessary.

The output interface is equally deliberate — the policy commands the platform exactly as a human FPV pilot does:

$$ a_t = (c,\; \omega_x,\; \omega_y,\; \omega_z) $$
Notation ours: \(c\) the collective thrust and \((\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z)\) the body rates — the abstract specifies that the system "leverages the same control commands humans use (collective thrust and body rates)".

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (verified at S5, 11 Jul 2026, from the full text): the "limited memory of the neural controller" — the policy sees only three past actions — is the named weakness; if no gates are visible for several frames the "success rate drops drastically" and the system cannot recover; future work names recurrent networks for "prolonged memory retention" and the sample efficiency of learning directly from pixels; broader applications are scoped as "indoor navigation or ship inspection" and "powerline inspection"; obstacle avoidance, dynamic objects and environment changes are unhandled.
  • MATERIAL CORRECTION from the S5 pass: the policy does not run on the drone — it runs on an "offboard ground-station PC" with commands returned to the vehicle via RF; the Swin gate detector runs in 4 ms on an RTX 3090 (offboard); video transmission latency is 33 ms at 60 Hz; the platform is a modified Agilicious carrying an onboard FPV camera only. Our earlier framing implied more onboard autonomy than the system has.
  • Verified detail: training is PPO in an "augmented simulator" — a data-driven augmentation of a quadratic propeller model; the gate detector was trained on 80,000 real + photorealistic images; an "initial state buffer" prevents catastrophic forgetting; domain randomization covers thrust, mass and gate positions; the figure-8 result is a 100% success rate across 6 runs and 20 flown laps.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Together with MonoRace, this paper bounds the perception design space for this project. MonoRace keeps state estimation and hardens it until it wins championships; Geles et al. remove it entirely and still fly at 40 km/h. Between those two poles sits the central perception decision the pilot experiment must take: estimate state and spend engineering on the estimator, or abstract the sensor and spend it on a detector. Practically, the training-time sensor abstraction is the cheapest credible route to vision-in-the-loop training on this project's RTX A4000 — no rendering farm required — and the policy's CTBR output interface matches the PX4 offboard rates mode already available on the Holybro X500, so the deployment plumbing is the same as for the state-based candidates.
