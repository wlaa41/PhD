HoST: humanoid standing-up control across diverse postures

Tao Huang, Junli Ren, Huayi Wang, Zirui Wang, Qingwei Ben, Muning Wen, Xiao Chen, Jianan Li & Jiangmiao Pang

TL;DR HoST teaches a 23-DoF Unitree G1 humanoid to stand up from diverse starting postures using reinforcement learning alone. It trains with PPO in Isaac Gym (4,096 parallel environments) using a multi-critic architecture over four reward groups (task, style, regularization, post-task), a vertical pull-force curriculum for exploration, and four terrain types (ground, platform, wall, slope) to diversify postures. An action rescaler that implicitly bounds motion speed and L2C2 smoothness regularisation keep the motion deployable, and domain randomisation (mass, CoM offset, gains, friction, 0–100 ms control delay) bridges to reality. The proprioception-only policy is deployed directly on the real G1 — no predefined trajectories, no sim-to-real adaptation — standing up indoors and outdoors and carrying payloads up to 12 kg.

1 · The problem

A humanoid robot that falls — or is deployed lying down — must stand up before it can do anything else, and it may find itself supine on flat ground, slumped against a wall, or draped over a platform. A single controller has to work across all of those starting postures, on real hardware, without a motion-capture rig telling it where its limbs are.

The classical route is to track a predefined standing-up trajectory, but a fixed trajectory assumes a fixed starting posture. HoST's claim is that posture-adaptive standing-up can be learned end-to-end: in the authors' words, real-world posture-adaptive motions are achieved “without relying on predefined trajectories or sim-to-real adaptation techniques”. The difficulty is threefold: exploration (random actions almost never discover a complete stand-up), reward design (getting up, looking natural and staying smooth are competing objectives at very different scales), and deployability (a policy that oscillates in simulation snaps real actuators).

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Proprioception in, PD targets out

The policy sees only what the real robot can sense: base angular velocity, roll and pitch, joint positions and velocities, its last action, and the action-rescale factor β — no motion capture, no vision. Because standing up is contact-rich and the G1 has no dedicated contact sensors, the authors “implicitly enhance contact detection by feeding the policy with the previous five states” (Section III-1). The output is a set of PD position targets tracked by a joint-level PD controller — the paper gives the standard torque law τt = Kp(pdt − pt) − Kdṗt, “where Kp and Kd represent the stiffness and damping coefficients” (Section III-2). The policy runs at 50 Hz; the PD loop runs at 200 Hz in simulation and 500 Hz on the real robot.

3.2 Four reward groups, four critics

Standing up mixes objectives that live at very different scales, so the reward is split into four groups — in the paper's words, “(1) task reward rtask … (2) style reward rstyle … (3) regularization reward rregu … and (4) post-task reward rpost” (Section IV-B) — combined as a weighted sum:

$$ r_t = w^{\text{task}} r_t^{\text{task}} + w^{\text{style}} r_t^{\text{style}} + w^{\text{regu}} r_t^{\text{regu}} + w^{\text{post}} r_t^{\text{post}} $$
The paper's overall reward (Section IV-B): a weighted sum over the four reward groups.

Rather than hand one critic the whole sum, HoST trains one critic per group and aggregates their GAE advantages after normalising each:

$$ A = \sum_i w^i \cdot \frac{A_{\phi_i} - \mu_{A_{\phi_i}}}{\sigma_{A_{\phi_i}}} $$
The paper's advantage aggregation (Section IV-B): each critic's GAE advantage is normalised, then weighted-summed.
Concept · why several critics beat one value head (ours)

A single critic must regress the sum of four reward streams whose magnitudes and time profiles differ wildly — a sparse task bonus, a dense style shaping term, small regularisation penalties, a post-task term that only matters once standing. Whichever stream is largest dominates the value target, and the others effectively vanish from the gradient. Giving each group its own critic and normalising the advantages puts every objective on the same footing before the weights \(w^i\) decide their influence — the ablation in Section 5 shows how much this matters. PPO is used underneath “because of its stability and efficiency in large-scale parallel training”.

3.3 Exploration: a pull-force curriculum and four terrains

Random exploration essentially never assembles a complete stand-up, so training scaffolds it: an upward force is applied to the robot's base — in the paper's words, “we apply an upward force F on the robot base … The force magnitude decreases progressively as the robot can maintain a target height” (Section IV-C). Posture diversity comes from the environment rather than from reference motions: “we design 4 terrains to diversify the starting postures: (1) ground that is flat, (2) platform that supports the trunk of robot, (3) wall that supports the trunk … and (4) slope” (Section IV-E1).

Concept · curriculum as a steadying hand (ours)

The pull force works like an adult steadying a toddler: early on it holds the trunk up so the policy experiences the upright part of the task at all and reward flows; as competence grows the help decays to zero, leaving a policy that stands unaided. It is curriculum learning applied to the physics rather than to the task specification — the environment itself gets harder.

3.4 Deployable motion: action rescaler β and L2C2 smoothness

Two mechanisms keep the learned motion safe for real actuators. First, “we introduce an action rescaler beta to scale the action output, implicitly controlling the bound of the maximal torques on each actuator” (Section IV-D1) — β shrinks over training, implicitly bounding motion speed. Second, the L2C2 smoothness regulariser is extended to the multi-critic setting: “This method applies regularization to both the actor-network and critics” (Section IV-D2):

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{L2C2}} = \lambda_\pi D(\pi_\theta(s_t), \pi_\theta(\bar{s}_t)) + \lambda_V \sum_i D(V_{\phi_i}(s_t), V_{\phi_i}(\bar{s}_t)) $$
The paper's L2C2 loss (Section IV-D2). Our reading of the symbols: \(\bar{s}_t\) is a perturbed neighbour of \(s_t\), so penalising the divergence \(D\) between outputs at nearby states makes both the actor and every critic locally smooth.
Concept · why smoothness is a sim-to-real mechanism (ours)

An RL policy left to its own devices exploits the simulator with high-frequency bang-bang actions that a real gearbox cannot track and that excite unmodelled dynamics. Making the policy's output (and its critics' value estimates) vary slowly with the state removes the incentive for such chatter at the source, which is cheaper and more principled than low-pass filtering the actions afterwards.

3.5 Sim-to-real: randomise the dynamics, then just deploy

The transfer story is deliberately plain: “we employ domain randomization [48] to bridge the physical gap between simulation and reality” (Section IV-E2). Table II of the paper randomises trunk mass, base CoM offset, link mass, friction, restitution, P/D gains, torque offset, motor strength, initial joint angles — and, notably for anyone deploying on latency-prone onboard compute, a control delay drawn from “U(0, 100) ms”. There is no adaptation module, no online system identification, no fine-tuning: the learned policies “are directly deployed on the Unitree G1 humanoid robot”.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): proprioception alone sufficed for many postures but caused some failures in outdoor tests, motivating environment perception (Section VIII); jointly training supine and prone starting postures degraded performance due to interference between sampled rollouts (Section VIII); integration of the standing-up controller with existing full humanoid systems is not demonstrated (Section VIII); and the simulated-versus-real joint-velocity discrepancy indicates a joint-torque gap, calling for more accurate actuator modelling (Section VI-B).
  • Our open questions (ours): how sensitive the method is to the four reward-group weights \(w^i\) and how much per-task tuning they need; training compute and the onboard deployment computer are not stated, so the recipe's cost is unknown; and whether the pull-force curriculum has an analogue outside contact-rich tasks — for a UAV, an assistive-force curriculum during aggressive recovery manoeuvres is an untested idea, not a claim.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Honest caveat first: this is a 23-DoF legged humanoid, not a UAV, and its central task — contact-rich standing-up — has no direct aerial equivalent, so the headline result does not transfer. What transfers are the mechanisms. The Isaac Gym + domain-randomisation + direct-deploy recipe with an explicit 0–100 ms control-delay randomisation is a concrete latency-robustness pattern for onboard Orin Nano inference on this project's X500 V2. The L2C2 smoothness regularisation and the action-rescaler speed bound are directly reusable for keeping zero-shot policies non-oscillatory on real actuators — the ablation showing oscillation-induced real-world failures is exactly the failure mode a quadrotor policy must avoid. And the multi-critic reward decomposition is a useful design whenever a UAV task mixes competing objectives (tracking vs. smoothness vs. safety). The force curriculum is posture-specific and the least relevant piece here.
