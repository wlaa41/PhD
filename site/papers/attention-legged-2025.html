Attention-Based Map Encoding for Learning Generalized Legged Locomotion

Junzhe He, Chong Zhang, Fabian Jenelten, Ruben Grandia, Moritz Bächer & Marco Hutter

TL;DR Instead of feeding a locomotion policy the whole elevation map, this paper learns an attention-based map encoding conditioned on the robot's proprioception, trained as part of the end-to-end controller with reinforcement learning. The network "learns to focus on steppable areas for future footholds", giving learning-based control the foothold precision usually reserved for model-based planners. The same recipe trains controllers for a 12-DoF quadruped and a 23-DoF humanoid, both tested in the real world in challenging indoor and outdoor scenarios, "including ones unseen during training". Caveat: this is an arXiv draft explicitly prior to peer review.

1 · The problem

Perceptive legged locomotion has an attention problem in the literal sense: the elevation map is large, but at any instant only a few of its cells matter — the ones where the next feet must land. A policy that ingests the whole map indiscriminately wastes capacity on irrelevant terrain and loses the precision that sparse footholds demand.

The abstract frames the field as a three-way trade-off. Traditional model-based controllers "excel at planning on complex terrains" but "struggle with real-world uncertainties". Learning-based controllers are robust to those uncertainties "but often lack precision on terrains with sparse steppable areas". Hybrid methods buy robustness on sparse terrain by combining both, at the price of being "computationally demanding and constrained by the inherent limitations of model-based planners". The open question is whether a pure learning-based controller can recover planner-like foothold precision without inheriting a planner.

There is a second, quieter question underneath: does the answer depend on the body? The paper applies one recipe to two very different morphologies — a 12-DoF quadruped and a 23-DoF humanoid — which makes it a cross-embodiment data point as much as a locomotion one.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Attention over map tokens, queried by the body

The abstract commits to an "attention-based map encoding conditioned on robot proprioception". The canonical mechanism this implies is scaled dot-product attention, with the query derived from the robot's proprioceptive state and the keys and values from patches of the elevation map:

$$ \operatorname{Attention}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \operatorname{softmax}\!\Big( \frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}} \Big) V $$
Notation ours — this is the canonical scaled dot-product form, with \(Q\) from proprioception and \(K, V\) from map patches as the natural reading of "conditioned on robot proprioception". The paper's exact formulation (tokenisation of the map, number of heads, dimensions) awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why conditioning on body state selects footholds

The same terrain cell matters differently depending on how the robot is moving: a stone half a metre ahead is everything at a fast trot and irrelevant when standing still. An unconditioned map encoder must compress all cells equally well; letting proprioception form the query turns encoding into retrieval — "given my current motion, which cells could carry my next feet?" That is precisely the behaviour the authors report: the network "learns to focus on steppable areas for future footholds". A side benefit they highlight is interpretability — the attention pattern "offers a way to interpret the topographical perception of a neural network".

3.2 The end-to-end RL objective

The encoder is not pre-trained on foothold labels or supervised terrain data; it is "trained as part of the end-to-end controller using reinforcement learning". Attention therefore emerges from whatever maximises the locomotion return:

$$ J(\pi) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi} \Big[ \textstyle\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^{t}\, r(s_t, a_t) \Big] $$
Notation ours — the generic discounted-return objective. The reward terms, the RL algorithm and the training curriculum are not in the abstract and await the full-PDF pass.

Registry merge note: this paper's attention use is counted under the existing self-attention-sensor-fusion entry rather than a new method (merge judgement logged) — it is the same mechanism as FalconGym's adaptive input weighting, applied to a different sensory domain.

3.3 Cross-embodiment: one recipe, two morphologies

The abstract is precise here: "We have trained two controllers for a 12-DoF quadrupedal robot and a 23-DoF humanoid robot respectively". This is not one policy spanning both embodiments — it is one training recipe producing a separate controller per platform. That is still notable: foothold-precise perceptive locomotion recipes rarely survive contact with a second morphology unchanged. Whether the two controllers share the encoder architecture, hyperparameters or any weights, and what changed between the quadruped and humanoid configurations, awaits the full-PDF pass.

4 · Results in the real world

The abstract-level evidence: both controllers were "tested … in the real world under various challenging indoor and outdoor scenarios, including ones unseen during training", and the synthesised behaviours "exhibit robustness against uncertainties while enabling precise and agile traversal of sparse terrains". The pairing is the point — robustness is the learning-based controller's usual strength, precision on sparse footholds its usual weakness, and the claim is that attention over the map buys both at once.

No quantitative metrics appear in the abstract. Success rates, tracking errors, terrain difficulty measures and baseline comparisons are all unknown at this extraction stage and await the full-PDF pass.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors: the abstract itself lists no limitations; the arXiv comments field, however, flags the paper's status plainly — this is the "Original draft prior to peer review" and "Significant revisions and new materials are expected after formal publication release".
  • Ours: as a pre-review draft, every claim on this page carries extra uncertainty — results, framing and even the method description may change in the published version.
  • Ours: no quantitative metrics are available from the abstract; the strength of the precision-plus-robustness claim cannot be judged until the full-PDF pass.
  • Ours: the zero-shot badge rests on the "unseen during training" sentence; whether the authors frame this as zero-shot generalisation, and how far the unseen scenarios depart from the training distribution, is to be verified at S5.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

This is the strongest cross-embodiment evidence in the corpus so far: one recipe producing real-world controllers for two morphologies as different as a quadruped and a humanoid. That maps directly onto this project's drone-to-RoboMaster ambition — the question of whether a transfer recipe, rather than a single policy, is the right unit of generalisation. Beyond embodiment, the mechanism itself is a candidate for this project: attention-based input selection over a spatial map, queried by the vehicle's own state, is exactly the shape of a terrain-aware UAV landing module — "which cells could carry my touchdown?" is the aerial twin of "which cells could carry my next feet?"
