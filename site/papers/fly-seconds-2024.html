Learning to Fly in Seconds

Jonas Eschmann, Dario Albani & Giuseppe Loianno

NYU Agile Robotics and Perception Lab · IEEE RA-L 2024 · arXiv, v1 November 2023, v2 April 2024 · arXiv:2311.13081 zero-shot triage score 92 = 20+25+20+12+15 S5 verification pending designated I2 baseline

TL;DR A deep-RL quadrotor controller trained end-to-end in 18 seconds on a consumer-grade laptop, commanding motor RPMs directly with no cascaded control stack underneath, and deployed on a microcontroller with real-time guarantees aboard a Crazyflie nano quadrotor. The enabling contribution is a purpose-built simulator that runs about 5 months of simulated flight per second on a laptop GPU — and the code and simulator are open source.

1 · The problem

Reinforcement learning for flight control is usually sample-starved and simulator-bound. Policies need millions of environment interactions, general-purpose simulators deliver them slowly, and the resulting wall-clock training cost — hours to days per run — gates how fast anyone can iterate on rewards, observations or architectures.

There is a second, quieter problem. Most "learned" flight controllers sit on top of a cascaded PID stack: the policy outputs attitude or body-rate setpoints and conventional inner loops do the actual motor-level work. That hides the interesting control problem inside hand-tuned loops and caps how much the learned component can matter. Commanding motor RPMs directly removes the cascade — the policy owns the whole problem — but it also raises the transfer stakes, because there are no inner loops left to absorb model error between simulation and the real vehicle.

This paper attacks both at once: a simulator fast enough to make RL training nearly free, and a training recipe that makes direct-RPM policies survive the trip to real hardware.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 The training algorithm: PPO with a clipped surrogate

The policy is trained with Proximal Policy Optimization, maximising the clipped surrogate objective over trajectories collected in the simulator:

$$ L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[ \min\!\Big( \rho_t(\theta)\,\hat{A}_t,\; \operatorname{clip}\!\big(\rho_t(\theta),\, 1-\varepsilon,\, 1+\varepsilon\big)\,\hat{A}_t \Big) \right], \qquad \rho_t(\theta) \;=\; \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid o_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t \mid o_t)}. $$
Notation ours — this is the canonical PPO form; the paper's exact objective, hyperparameters and any modifications await the full-PDF pass.
Concept · what the clip buys you

PPO reuses each batch of experience for several gradient steps, so the policy being updated, \(\pi_\theta\), drifts away from the policy that collected the data, \(\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}\). The ratio \(\rho_t\) measures that drift per action. Left unchecked, a large ratio lets one batch push the policy arbitrarily far, and performance collapses. The clip removes any incentive to move \(\rho_t\) outside \([1-\varepsilon,\, 1+\varepsilon]\): beyond that band the objective goes flat, so gradients vanish and the update stays near the data-collecting policy. It is a cheap, first-order stand-in for a trust region — exactly the property you want when training must finish in seconds rather than hours.

3.2 Asymmetric actor–critic: let the critic cheat

The architecture is asymmetric: the critic and the actor see different worlds. The critic is trained on privileged simulator state, while the actor is restricted to observations the real vehicle will actually have at deployment:

$$ V_\phi\big(s_t^{\text{priv}}\big) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}\Big[ \textstyle\sum_{k \ge 0} \gamma^{k}\, r_{t+k} \;\Big|\; s_t^{\text{priv}} \Big], \qquad a_t \sim \pi_\theta\big(\cdot \mid o_t\big). $$
Notation ours — \(s_t^{\text{priv}}\) the privileged simulator state, \(o_t\) the deployable observation. The paper's exact observation and state definitions await the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why the critic may cheat

The critic's only job is to estimate values that reduce the variance of the actor's policy gradient during training — and it is discarded at deployment. It can therefore be fed anything the simulator knows (exact velocities, disturbance terms, motor states) without breaking transfer, because nothing privileged ever reaches the network that ships. A better-informed critic gives cleaner advantage estimates, which means fewer samples and shorter training; the actor, meanwhile, is forced from the first gradient step to work only with what the Crazyflie can measure. The asymmetry is a free lunch precisely because the two networks have different lifespans.

3.3 Curriculum learning

Training follows a curriculum: the task distribution starts easy and is stepped towards the full problem as the policy improves, which keeps early gradients informative instead of drowning them in failures. Schematically, tasks are drawn from a sequence of distributions indexed by a curriculum stage \(k\):

$$ \text{task} \sim p_k(\text{task}), \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \dots $$
Notation ours — the abstract confirms that curriculum learning is used; what exactly is stepped (initial-state spread, reward terms, trajectory difficulty) and on what schedule awaits the full-PDF pass.

3.4 The simulator is the real contribution

The headline number is 18 seconds of training, but the load-bearing number is the simulator's throughput: about 5 months of simulated flight per second on a laptop GPU. At that rate the 18-second run corresponds to roughly seven and a half years of flight experience (our arithmetic from the two stated figures). The point is economic: for multirotor RL, sample cost — not algorithm novelty — was the binding constraint, and once samples are effectively free, an entirely standard algorithm (PPO) with two careful engineering choices (asymmetry, curriculum) is sufficient. The simulator is purpose-built and optimised for this workload rather than general-purpose, and it is open-sourced, which is what makes the result reproducible rather than merely impressive.

The deployment claim deserves emphasis: the policy runs on a microcontroller with real-time guarantees, not on a companion computer. For a direct-RPM controller this is the difference between a demo and a controller — missed deadlines at the motor level are crashes, not latency.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors: none listed in the abstract; the limitations section awaits the full-PDF extraction pass.
  • Our open questions (marked as ours, to be checked against the full text): validation in the abstract is Crazyflie-only — does the recipe scale to 500 g+ platforms with brushless motors and ESCs, where the RPM interface is mediated by another controller? How do direct-RPM policies cope with battery sag, which shifts the RPM-to-thrust map over a single flight? And the zero-shot wording: the abstract claims sim-to-real transfer and deployment, but whether transfer is strictly without any real-world fine-tuning is to be confirmed at S5.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

This is the project's designated I2 reproduction baseline — the paper whose numbers we reproduce, not merely cite. Open code, tiny compute and a purpose-built simulator make it the fastest honest route from a simulator install to a tethered flight, which is precisely the arc the transfer report's Chapter 4 needs. The open-source simulator and checkpoints de-risk the A4000 training stage: if 18 seconds on a consumer laptop is real, training cost disappears as a risk from the project plan entirely. Its microcontroller deployment path maps directly onto this project's Pixhawk 6X / Orin Nano split — low-level control on the flight controller, everything else as headroom. Reproducing its training-time and tracking numbers is the current headline candidate for Chapter 4 of the transfer report.
