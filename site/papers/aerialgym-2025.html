Aerial Gym Simulator: GPU-parallel aerial robotics

Mihir Kulkarni, Welf Rehberg & Kostas Alexis

TL;DR A modular, GPU-parallel simulator for arbitrary multirotors — under-, fully- and over-actuated — built on NVIDIA Isaac Gym. Physics scales to 65,536 parallel instances at 4.43 million samples per second on a single RTX 3090, and a custom ray-casting renderer delivers depth, segmentation and vertex-level annotations an order of magnitude faster than Isaac Gym's default engine. Policies trained inside it — including depth-based navigation through a 64-dimensional collision-encoder latent — are deployed on real drones without additional fine-tuning. Open-sourced.

1 · The problem

Learning-based flight control is bottlenecked by simulation infrastructure. RL needs millions of environment samples, and vision-based policies additionally need rendered sensor data at that scale — which is precisely where existing aerial simulators are slow.

The paper's own benchmark makes the gap concrete: gym-pybullet-drones renders at 5.13–16.70 FPS, and even OmniDrones' native renderer reaches 219–307 FPS — orders of magnitude short of what massively parallel RL consumes. A second gap is generality: most drone simulators assume a standard planar quadrotor, which shuts out fully- and over-actuated multirotor research. Aerial Gym's pitch is infrastructural rather than algorithmic: "a highly parallelized, modular framework for simulation and rendering of arbitrary multirotor platforms based on NVIDIA Isaac Gym", with the whole training-to-deployment loop — controllers, sensors, sim-to-real matching, example tasks — packaged and open-sourced.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 A manager-based, modular architecture

Aerial Gym is organised around managers: "the managers for the rendering engine, robots and environments, interfaces for the physics engine and common DRL frameworks along with the definition of learning tasks" (Section III-A). Robots are defined parametrically — inertias come from CAD — and the framework accepts under-, fully- and over-actuated multirotor configurations rather than assuming a standard quadrotor. Physics runs through NVIDIA Isaac Gym, scaled to 2¹⁶ (65,536) parallel instances.

3.2 Parallelised geometric controllers at every abstraction level

The simulator ships controllers "across levels of abstraction based on the taxonomy in [33]" (Section III-C): attitude-thrust, position/velocity/acceleration setpoints, body-wrench, and direct RPM/thrust interfaces, resolved to per-motor commands via pseudoinverse allocation. The position controller's thrust command is the standard geometric form (Section III-C2):

$$ f = (k_x e_x + k_v e_v + m g e_3 - m \ddot{x}_d) \cdot R e_3 $$
From the paper (Section III-C2): \(e_x, e_v\) are position and velocity errors in the inertial frame, \(k_x, k_v\) the respective gains.

The attitude controller's desired body-torque (Section III-C1):

$$ M = -k_R e_R - k_\Omega e_\Omega + \Omega \times J \Omega $$
From the paper (Section III-C1): \(e_R\) the orientation error, \(e_\Omega\) the angular-rate error, expressed in the body frame.

At the lowest level, motors follow first-order RPM dynamics with a direction-dependent time constant — "the motor-time constant is tau_k and takes the value tau_k^dec in case of slowing down or tau_k^inc otherwise" (Section III-C4):

$$ \dot{r}_k = \frac{1}{\tau_k}\left(r_k^{ref} - r_k\right),\qquad \tau_k = \begin{cases}\tau_k^{inc} & r_k^{ref} \ge r_k \\ \tau_k^{dec} & r_k^{ref} \lt r_k\end{cases} $$
From the paper (Section III-C4). This is the interface a direct motor-command policy acts through.
Concept · why ship controllers with a simulator?

An RL researcher rarely wants to learn everything from torque upwards. By providing classical geometric controllers at each abstraction level — vectorised across tens of thousands of environments — the framework lets a policy choose its action space: command positions and let the stack do the rest, or bypass everything and drive motor RPMs directly. The same taxonomy of interfaces also becomes the sim-to-real contract: whatever level the policy commands in simulation is the level it commands on the real flight stack. This framing is ours; the interface list is the paper's.

3.3 GPU ray-casting sensors and a deliberately noisy IMU

Exteroception is rendered by custom GPU kernels: "individual rays are cast outwards per-pixel to evaluate intersection" (Section III-D1), producing depth, segmentation and vertex-level annotations. The authors report "an order of magnitude speedup compared to the default rendering engine of Isaac Gym with support for newer data types such as point clouds and surface normals". Proprioception is deliberately imperfect: "the simulated IMU utilizes the true forces measured by the simulator alongside a Gaussian noise and a discrete-time bias random-walk implementation" (Section III-D2).

Concept · rendering is the real bottleneck for vision RL

Physics engines were made fast years ago; what starves vision-based RL is producing camera frames for tens of thousands of environments at every step. A ray-caster that only answers "where does this ray hit the mesh?" skips the general-purpose rasterisation pipeline, which is why it can be an order of magnitude faster — and why depth, rather than RGB, is the natural first-class sensor here. This explanation is ours; the speedup claim and the sensor list are the paper's.

3.4 Learning tasks, system identification, zero-shot deployment

Example tasks follow the Gymnasium standard, trained with RL Games and Sample Factory. The flagship is depth-based navigation, where "z_t is a 64-dimensional representation obtained from a 848x480 resolution depth image using the DCE" — the Deep Collision Encoder — so the policy sees a compact collision-relevant latent rather than raw pixels (Section V-B). The sim-to-real recipe is notably lightweight: "the robot inertias are obtained using CAD, while the response of the simulated controllers and motor models is matched with real-world system" (Section III-E), plus the IMU noise-and-bias model above — system identification and sensor-noise modelling rather than heavy domain randomisation. Deployment is then direct: "trained policies are directly deployed on real-world robots without additional fine-tuning to evaluate their performance."

Note (ours): the paper displays no reward-function equations for either RL task — only qualitative problem statements and state vectors — so none are reproduced here.

Two attribution points matter (corrected during verification): the 142-minute training time belongs to the vision-based navigation task (Section V-B), while the 250 Hz inference rate and 0.01 s control time-step describe the separate low-level motor-control task (Section V-C) — they are not one experiment. And the real-world material is thin on metrics: our extraction records no tracking-error, success-rate or flight-count figures for the hardware experiments. The transfer claim at this pass rests on the abstract's qualitative statement that "extensive simulation studies are conducted and successful sim2real transfer of trained policies is demonstrated", with deployment "without additional fine-tuning".

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): the adapted geometric controllers have sub-optimal performance on non-planar (non-standard) multirotor configurations and are offered only as a starting point for future research (Section III-C1); and the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse control-allocation scheme is suboptimal for systems with input constraints — a common approximation the authors note can be replaced with custom strategies (Section III-C3). The paper has no dedicated limitations section; these are the only shortcomings explicitly flagged, and no future-work roadmap beyond "serving as a starting point for future research" appears in the fetched full text.
  • Our open questions (ours): how far the lightweight sim-to-real recipe (system-ID matching plus IMU noise modelling, without heavy domain randomisation) stretches once a real platform drifts from its CAD model; what quantitative tracking error the deployed policies actually achieve, since the paper reports none; whether a DCE latent trained on simulated ray-cast depth survives real D455 depth artefacts (holes, noise floors) beyond the environments demonstrated; and how much of the framework carries forward given Isaac Gym's deprecation in favour of newer NVIDIA stacks.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Of the infrastructure papers in this corpus, Aerial Gym is the closest match to this project's training-to-deployment loop: a GPU-parallel multirotor simulator with a fast depth renderer and a demonstrated zero-shot depth-based navigation policy on real drones — exactly the pattern intended for the X500 V2 + Orin Nano platform. The mapping is unusually direct: its RealSense D455 + depth-latent (DCE) pipeline transplants onto a depth-camera-equipped X500, and the ArduPilot-based deployment platform plus open-source code lower the barrier to reproducing sim2real navigation. It is equally valuable as a contrast point: the sim-to-real recipe here is lightweight — system-ID matching of controller/motor response plus IMU noise-and-bias modelling, not heavy domain randomisation — a clean baseline for this project's gap analysis of how much randomisation zero-shot transfer actually needs. No platform-mismatch caveat applies: this is a drone paper deployed on drone hardware.
