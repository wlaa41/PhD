Agile But Safe: collision-free high-speed legged locomotion

Tairan He, Chong Zhang, Wenli Xiao, Guanqi He, Changliu Liu & Guanya Shi

TL;DR A quadruped that runs fast and does not hit things. ABS pairs an agile goal-reaching policy with a recovery policy, switched in closed loop by a learned control-theoretic reach-avoid (RA) value network. All four modules — agile policy, RA value network, recovery policy and a ResNet-18 ray-prediction perception net — are trained in Isaac Gym (1,280 parallel environments) and deployed on a Unitree Go1 with onboard sensing and compute, with no fine-tuning: 3.1 m/s peak in the real world (4.0 m/s in simulation), 2.5 m/s peak amid dynamic adversarial obstacles, and 9–10/10 success across real environments.

1 · The problem

Legged robots have historically had to choose between speed and safety. The abstract puts it directly: "Existing studies either develop conservative controllers (< 1.0 m/s) to ensure safety, or focus on agility without considering potentially fatal collisions."

The gap is structural, not incremental: an agile RL policy has no notion of when it is about to fail, and a formally safe controller is safe partly because it is slow. ABS's claim is that the trade-off can be broken by keeping the agile policy aggressive and delegating safety to a separate, learned mechanism — a learned control-theoretic reach-avoid value that watches the agile policy and takes over only when its estimate says the current course ends in collision. The whole system must also run on the robot itself: "These trained modules can be directly deployed in the real world with onboard sensing and computation, leading to high-speed and collision-free navigation."

2 · The approach at a glance

The idea in one sentence: let an aggressive policy drive, let a learned reach-avoid value function referee, and hand control to a specialist recovery policy the moment the referee says the current course could end in a collision.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Four modules, one closed loop

ABS is not a single network but a system of four: an agile locomotion policy, a reach-avoid value network, a recovery policy and a ray-prediction perception network. The switching logic is the paper's core architectural move: "The policy switch in ABS is governed by a learned control-theoretic reach-avoid value network, which also guides the recovery policy as an objective function". Each module is trained separately in simulation, and "All of the four modules are directly deployed in the real world after training with onboard sensing and computation" — the zero-shot claim covers the whole stack, not just the locomotion policy.

3.2 The agile policy: goal-reaching, not velocity-tracking

The agile policy is trained with PPO on a goal-reaching task rather than the more common velocity-tracking formulation, using "the GPU-based Isaac Gym simulator which supports us to train 1280 environments in parallel with the PPO algorithm". Two randomisation choices carry the sim-to-real crossing. For dynamics, "ERFI-50 proposed by Campanaro et al. implicitly models the motor sim-to-real gaps with random torque perturbations". For the environment side of the observation, a mechanism the authors call illusion "makes the policy more robust to unseen geometries such as walls: it overwrites observed ray distances by random values" — the policy learns not to trust any single ray reading too much.

3.3 The reach-avoid value network

After the agile policy is trained, it is rolled out to collect a dataset of roughly 200k episodes labelled by outcome, and a value network \(\hat V\) is fitted to the discounted reach-avoid Bellman equation:

$$ V^{\pi}_{RA}(s) = \gamma_{RA}\,\max\{\zeta(s),\ \min\{l(s),\ V^{\pi}_{RA}(f(s,\pi(s)))\}\} + (1-\gamma_{RA})\,\max\{l(s),\zeta(s)\} $$
The paper's Eq. 5 (Sec. III-B4), transcribed from the ar5iv HTML render — symbol-level notation may carry minor transcription artefacts. The discount \(\gamma_{RA}\) makes the otherwise-undiscounted RA fixed point learnable. The precise definitions of \(l(s)\) and \(\zeta(s)\) were not captured verbatim in our extraction, so we do not restate them here (unverified detail, labelled).
Concept · what a reach-avoid value is (ours)

In reach-avoid problems from control theory, a value function summarises, for each state, a question with two clauses: starting here and following this policy, will the system reach its target set without ever entering the failure set? Unlike an ordinary RL value that sums reward, the RA value is built from max/min operations over trajectories, so its sign (relative to a threshold) acts as a certificate-like predicate — "safe to continue" versus "on course to fail" — rather than a graded score. ABS's twist is to learn this value for the learned agile policy from rollout data, instead of computing it from a model, which is what lets it work with an otherwise opaque neural controller.

3.4 The recovery policy and the runtime shield

The recovery policy is a second RL policy with a narrower job: it "is trained to track twist commands rapidly from high-speed movements … to make the robot track a given twist command as fast as possible". At runtime the RA value arbitrates between the two policies in closed loop: "If V̂ ≥ V_threshold, [the recovery policy takes control]; If V̂ < V_threshold, the agile policy takes control." When the recovery policy is in charge, its twist command is not hand-picked — it comes from a constrained optimisation over the RA value itself:

$$ tw^{c} = \arg\min\ d^{future}_{goal}\quad \text{s.t.}\quad \hat{V}([tw^{c};\,G^{c}_{x,y};\,R]) < V_{threshold} $$
The paper's Eq. 21 (Sec. V-C), transcribed from the ar5iv HTML render: choose the twist that minimises the predicted future distance to the goal, subject to the RA value staying below the safety threshold. The paper reports this optimisation converging within 5 steps.
Concept · why shield with a learned value (ours)

Classic safety shields (control barrier functions, Hamilton–Jacobi reachability) need a dynamics model, which is exactly what an end-to-end RL system does not expose. By fitting the RA value to rollouts of the actual trained policy, ABS gets a shield calibrated to what the agile policy will really do — including its mistakes — and because \(\hat V\) is differentiable, the same network doubles as the objective landscape the recovery optimisation descends. One network, two roles: tripwire and steering signal.

3.5 Perception: eleven rays instead of pixels

Neither policy consumes raw images. Instead "we choose to use a low-dimensional exteroception representation: the distances that 11 rays travel from the robot to obstacles, similar to sparse LiDAR readings" — but there is no LiDAR on the robot. The rays are predicted from the stereo camera: "We finetune ResNet-18 with pretrained weights to train the model. The images are downsampled to [160,90] resolution". The same 11-ray abstraction is the observation for both the agile policy and the RA value network, so perception errors hit the policy and its referee identically. State estimation is deliberately minimal: "IMU-based orientation estimation for g (i.e., roll and pitch) is usually very accurate", and the authors report that "the policy can effectively handle the odometry drift".

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): dense obstacle layouts defeat the policy — "when the obstacles are too dense and form a local minimum, our policy can easily fail, which is also reflected by the high timeout rates"; "The RA values are learned with static obstacles, and can only generalize to quasi-static environments"; "If a dynamic object moves faster than the velocity limit of the recovery policy, the collision may happen"; "we limit the robot behaviors to only 2D locomotion and constrain the motions to have no flying phase"; "For 3D terrains such as stairs and gaps, the problem can be far more challenging because the locomotion skills and collision avoidance are coupled"; "implicit system identification techniques can leverage temporal information … but it is non-trivial to incorporate them into our system"; and in the real-world trials "the only collision of ABS is due to the 'undetected' objects by the ray-prediction network as the corridor is quite dim".
  • No explicit future-work list: the Conclusion (Sec. X) gives three takeaways — integrating locomotion and navigation, low-dimensional exteroception, and breaking the agility–safety trade-off via external shielding — but states no explicit future-work sentence. Material for this section beyond the failure-case discussion is therefore thin, and we say so rather than pad it.
  • Our open questions (ours): how sensitive the whole shield is to the single scalar \(V_{threshold}\), and how it was tuned; whether 11 rays remain enough at speeds where obstacles enter and leave the narrow predicted fan between control decisions; what the RA formulation looks like for a vehicle that cannot brake to a stop in place (a multirotor must keep flying while "recovering"); and whether the dim-corridor failure implies the shield needs an uncertainty estimate on perception, not just on dynamics.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Platform mismatch first: ABS is a quadruped paper, its behaviours are explicitly restricted to 2D locomotion with no flying phase, and nothing here was demonstrated on an aerial or wheeled platform — so no result transfers directly to this project's X500 V2 quadrotor or RoboMaster EP. What does transfer is the architecture. First, the perception abstraction: distilling a stereo depth camera into 11 "sparse-LiDAR" ray distances through a small ResNet-18 is a concrete recipe for cutting onboard perception cost, and the Go1's ZED Mini + Jetson Orin NX stack is a near-twin of this project's Orin Nano setup. Second, the safety pattern: a learned reach-avoid value shielding an aggressive policy, with a recovery fallback chosen by constrained optimisation over that same value, maps naturally onto emergency avoidance for a fast UAV — and is the corpus's cleanest worked example of safety added around an RL policy rather than baked into its reward. Third, the state-estimation frugality (trust IMU roll/pitch, tolerate odometry drift) matches how a GPS-denied UAV must fly. Before any port: the RA values are static-obstacle-only, fast movers can still defeat the recovery policy, and the RA formulation would need re-deriving for full 3D dynamics where "stop and recover" is not an available action.
