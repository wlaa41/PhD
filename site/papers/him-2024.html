HIM: agile locomotion via a hybrid internal model

Junfeng Long, Zirui Wang, Quanyi Li, Jiawei Gao, Liu Cao & Jiangmiao Pang

TL;DR HIM treats everything a legged robot cannot sense — terrain friction, elevation, pushes — as disturbances in the classical internal-model-control sense, and estimates them from the robot's own response using only joint encoders and an IMU. A history-conditioned 3-layer MLP produces a "hybrid internal embedding": an explicit velocity estimate regressed to simulator ground truth, plus a 16-dimensional implicit latent trained with a SwAV-style contrastive loss that pulls it toward the successor state. Training in Isaac Gym takes ~1,000 rollouts, about one hour on a single RTX 4090; the same policy then runs on real Unitree A1, Go1 and Aliengo quadrupeds at 50 Hz, handling terrains and disturbance cases "never occurred during the training process".

1 · The problem

A quadruped's hardest inputs are the ones it cannot measure. Terrain friction, ground elevation and external pushes shape every footstep, yet the deployed robot sees only its own proprioception — "those from joint encoders and IMU" (Abstract).

The dominant recipe for this gap — the teacher–student, privileged-learning family that HIM benchmarks against (RMA) — trains a teacher with simulator-only information and then trains a deployable student to mimic it. The abstract's stated objection is that this mismatch loses information: HIM "innovatively maintains consistent observations between simulation reference and reality that avoids information loss in mimicking learning" (Abstract). The question the paper answers is whether a robot can estimate what it cannot see purely from how its own body responds — no teacher, no privileged observation channel, no camera.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 External states become disturbances

HIM's framing move is to stop trying to reconstruct the environment and instead treat it as a disturbance acting on the robot's body. The estimator watches how the body responds: "HIM uses sequential history observations to extract the hybrid internal embedding that consists of the robot's velocity and the implicit response" (§3.2). The word hybrid is doing real work — one half of the embedding is a physically meaningful quantity, the other is a free latent.

Concept · internal model control (ours)

In classical control, a regulator can only reject a disturbance it can model — the internal model principle. HIM imports this into RL: the network carries an internal model of "what is pushing me", split into an explicit part it can be supervised on (base velocity, whose ground truth the simulator knows) and an implicit part that has no label at all (everything else — friction, slope, a shove). The design question then becomes: how do you train a latent that has no label?

3.2 The estimator: one small MLP over history

The deployable policy sees only what the real robot will have: "the desired velocity, the proprioceptive information from its joint encoder and IMU, and the last action" (§3.1). The estimator consumes a stacked history of these observations; "the extractor is a 3-layer Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) with hidden dimensions of 512, 256, and 128, respectively" (§3.2). Its output splits into the explicit velocity estimate and a 16-dimensional implicit stability latent \(\mathbf{l}_t\). No recurrence, no teacher network — a plain MLP over a window of the recent past.

3.3 Hybrid Internal Optimization: a label and a contrast

The explicit half is straightforward: in simulation the true base velocity is known, so "we can directly learn to regress the explicit velocity \(\hat{v}_t\) using its ground truth" (§3.3). The implicit half is where the paper earns its title: "the hybrid internal embedding is optimized through contrastive learning, which pulls close the embedding and the future state" (§3.3). Both of the paper's equations concern this contrastive step. First, latents are softly assigned to a set of prototype vectors \(\mathbf{e}_k\):

$$ \mathbf{p}_t^{\text{source}} = \dfrac{\exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\tau}\,\mathbf{l}_t^{\text{source}\top}\mathbf{e}_k\right)}{\sum_{k'}\exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\tau}\,\mathbf{l}_t^{\text{source}\top}\mathbf{e}_{k'}\right)} $$
The paper's Eq. (1), as rendered in §3.3 of the arXiv HTML full text: a softmax cluster assignment of the latent over prototype vectors \(\mathbf{e}_k\) with temperature \(\tau\).
$$ \mathcal{J}^{\text{SwAV}} = -\dfrac{1}{2H}\sum_{t=1}^{H}\left(\mathbf{q}_t^{\text{source}}\log\mathbf{p}_t^{\text{target}} + \mathbf{q}_t^{\text{target}}\log\mathbf{p}_t^{\text{source}}\right) $$
The paper's Eq. (2), the SwAV swapped-prediction loss. Symbol reading ours: "source" is the history-derived latent, "target" the successor state's embedding, \(\mathbf{q}\) the corresponding target assignment codes over the same prototypes, and \(H\) the horizon of the sum.
Concept · why a swapped prediction instead of a teacher (ours)

SwAV-style contrastive learning never compares embeddings directly. Both views — here, the latent built from the past and the embedding of the state that comes next — are assigned to shared prototype clusters, and each view must predict the other's assignment. If a latent computed purely from history can predict which cluster the future state lands in, it has captured how the world is currently pushing the robot — which is exactly the disturbance estimate the controller needs. The supervision is free (the successor state arrives one step later in simulation), so nothing privileged is baked in and nothing is lost at deployment, unlike mimicking a privileged teacher.

3.4 Training: two optimisers taking turns, one hour total

"The optimization process encompasses two phases: Hybrid Internal Optimization (HIO), which trains the hybrid internal model, and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), which optimizes the policy network" (§3.1). Everything runs in simulation: "We use Isaac Gym (Rudin et al., 2022) with 4096 parallel environments" (§3.4). Robustness comes from dynamics randomisation — "we randomize the mass of the robot body and links, the centre of mass, the payload, the ground friction and restitution coefficients, the motor strength, the joint-level PD gains, the system delay, the external force, and the initial joint positions" (§3.4) — note that randomising the system delay is the paper's whole answer to latency. Terrain difficulty follows a curriculum: "We create a height field map with 200 terrains arranged in a 20x10 grid, and each row has the same type of terrain arranged in increasing difficulty…The terrain level will increase if the robot reaches 80% of linear tracking reward" (§3.4). The budget is the headline: "The training process needs 1000 rollouts which takes 1 hour of wall clock time on NVIDIA RTX 4090" (§3.4).

3.5 Deployment: three robots, one policy stack

"We deploy our policy on Unitree A1, Go1, and Aliengo. The policy runs at 50 Hz, with a PD controller running at 500 Hz to track the target" (§4.1). Actions are position targets handed to the high-rate PD loop, and the whole stack needs nothing beyond proprioception: "It only needs the robot's proprioceptions, i.e., those from joint encoders and IMU as observations" (Abstract).

4 · Results on real hardware

The paper's claim is scoped by its abstract: "It only requires 1 hour of training on an RTX 4090 to enable a quadruped robot to traverse any terrain under any disturbances", with real-world experiments showing agility "even in high-difficulty tasks and cases never occurred during the training process, revealing remarkable open-world generalizability" (Abstract).

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): relying on proprioception alone bounds the skill ceiling — "the lack of external perceptions, e.g., visual information, leaves legged robots with only a primitive skill set" (Conclusion) — and the stated future work is to fix exactly that: "We will incorporate our method with external perception in the future to overcome more challenging environments and higher-level tasks" (Conclusion).
  • Our open questions (ours): whether a 16-dimensional implicit latent saturates under compound disturbances (payload and slippery slope and push at once); whether the contrastive successor-state objective still yields a useful latent for aerial dynamics, where the "stability" the latent encodes means something different; how sensitive the one-hour recipe is to the reward and curriculum settings it inherits from the Isaac Gym legged-locomotion stack; and how HIM's implicit estimate compares head-to-head against a recurrent in-context adapter (RAPTOR-style) rather than only against privileged distillation (RMA). 6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Platform mismatch first: this is a proprioception-only quadruped paper (Unitree A1/Go1/Aliengo), not a UAV work, so nothing transfers verbatim to this project's X500 V2 / Orin Nano / RoboMaster EP hardware. What transfers is conceptual, and it is strong. (a) Treating unobservable external states as disturbances estimated from the robot's own response maps naturally onto a UAV: wind, payload shifts and ground effect all leave signatures in IMU and motor response, no extra sensor required. (b) The SwAV-style successor-state objective is a label-free alternative to privileged teacher–student distillation — a cheap candidate for a UAV adaptation module that avoids the observation mismatch this corpus keeps running into. (c) The budget is the quiet headline: Isaac Gym + PPO, roughly one hour on a single RTX 4090, is a realistic training loop for a solo PhD. One honesty caveat carried from the extraction: the paper never claims "zero-shot" in those words, so in the gap analysis its zero-shot status should be cited as inferred, not quoted.
