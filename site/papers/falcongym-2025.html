TL;DR Instead of making a simulator's physics more faithful, FalconGym makes its pictures more faithful. A Neural Radiance Field reconstructs the real race track photorealistically, giving an effectively unlimited supply of synthetic camera images. A vision-based navigation policy — a Neural Pose Estimator fused with an IMU through a Kalman filter, feeding a self-attention controller that adaptively weighs visual features against pose estimates — is trained entirely in simulation by imitation learning, then flown on real hardware with no fine-tuning: 95.8% success over 30 live flights, ~10 cm average gate error, ~120 gates crossed on three track shapes.

1 · The problem

Sim-to-real transfer for vision is harder than for dynamics. A dynamics gap can be narrowed by randomising masses, motor constants and disturbances; a rendering gap cannot be randomised away so easily, because the input to the policy is the image itself.

A policy that consumes pixels learns the statistics of the pixels it was trained on. Classic game-engine simulators produce images whose textures, lighting and lens behaviour differ systematically from a real onboard camera, so a visually trained policy meets an out-of-distribution input the moment it leaves simulation — before any dynamics mismatch has a chance to matter. The usual remedies are expensive: collect real flight data for fine-tuning, or randomise appearance so aggressively that the policy discards most visual information.

FalconGym's position is that photorealism attacks the perception half of the gap directly. If the simulator is built from real photographs of the target environment — a radiance field fitted to the actual track — then synthetic training images are statistically close to what the real camera will deliver, and the visual front-end need never notice that it has crossed into reality. The dynamics half of the problem is then handled by estimating pose and filtering it, rather than by learning raw-image-to-motor mappings.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 A radiance field as the simulator's camera

The environment — gates, track, background — is reconstructed as a Neural Radiance Field fitted to real photographs. A NeRF represents the scene as a volumetric function returning density \(\sigma\) and view-dependent colour \(\mathbf{c}\) at every point; the colour of a camera ray \(\mathbf{r}\) is then obtained by volume rendering:

$$ C(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; \int_{t_n}^{t_f} T(t)\,\sigma\big(\mathbf{r}(t)\big)\, \mathbf{c}\big(\mathbf{r}(t),\mathbf{d}\big)\,\mathrm{d}t, \qquad T(t) \;=\; \exp\!\Big(-\!\int_{t_n}^{t}\sigma\big(\mathbf{r}(s)\big)\,\mathrm{d}s\Big) $$
Notation ours (canonical NeRF volume-rendering form): \(\mathbf{r}(t)\) a point along the ray, \(\mathbf{d}\) the viewing direction, \(T(t)\) accumulated transmittance. The paper's exact rendering formulation awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why a radiance field gives unlimited training views

Once \(\sigma\) and \(\mathbf{c}\) have been fitted from a finite set of posed photographs, the scene can be rendered from any camera pose, not only the poses that were photographed. The training-image budget is therefore decoupled from real flight time: every simulated trajectory yields photorealistic frames along it, with the ground-truth pose of each frame known exactly — which is precisely the labelled data a pose estimator needs, at a scale no manual collection could match.

3.2 Neural Pose Estimator, stabilised by a Kalman filter

The Neural Pose Estimator (NPE) maps an RGB frame to an estimate of the quadrotor's pose relative to the track. Any per-frame estimator is noisy and can fail outright when the gate leaves the field of view, so the NPE output is fused with IMU-driven odometry in a Kalman filter — the IMU propagates the state between frames, the NPE corrects it:

$$ \begin{aligned} \hat{x}_k^- &= F\,\hat{x}_{k-1}, &\qquad P_k^- &= F\,P_{k-1}\,F^{\mathsf{T}} + Q,\\[3pt] K_k &= P_k^-\,H^{\mathsf{T}}\big(H\,P_k^-\,H^{\mathsf{T}} + R\big)^{-1}, &\qquad \hat{x}_k &= \hat{x}_k^- + K_k\big(z_k - H\,\hat{x}_k^-\big). \end{aligned} $$
Notation ours (standard predict–update form): \(F\) the IMU-driven motion model, \(z_k\) the NPE pose treated as the measurement, \(Q\) and \(R\) the process and measurement noise covariances. The paper's exact filter design awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · fusing a learned pose with IMU odometry

The trick is architectural, not mathematical: the learned network is slotted into the filter as the measurement \(z_k\), while the IMU supplies the prediction. The covariance \(R\) encodes how much the vision estimate is trusted — when the NPE is confident the correction dominates; when the visual estimate degrades, the filter coasts on inertial propagation. Neither sensor alone suffices: the IMU drifts without bound, and the camera drops out whenever the gate is off-frame or blurred. Fused, each covers the other's failure mode.

3.3 A self-attention controller over two modalities

The controller does not consume a fixed concatenation of inputs. Visual features and filtered pose estimates enter a self-attention block, which computes data-dependent weights over them:

$$ \mathrm{Attention}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}\!\Big(\frac{Q K^{\mathsf{T}}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big)\, V $$
Notation ours (canonical scaled dot-product form): \(Q\), \(K\), \(V\) the query, key and value projections of the multi-modal input tokens. The paper's exact controller architecture awaits the full-PDF pass.

The point of attention here is adaptive weighting under degradation. When the gate is centred and sharp, visual features carry the most task-relevant signal and receive the weight; when the view degrades — motion blur, the gate slipping out of frame mid-turn — the softmax shifts weight towards the Kalman-filtered pose estimate, which remains smooth through the dropout. A fixed fusion scheme would have to compromise between the two regimes; attention lets the controller choose per time-step. The policy itself is trained by imitation learning against expert trajectories, entirely inside the NeRF simulator — no reward function is designed, and no real image is seen before deployment.

4 · Results on real hardware

The evaluation is a genuine zero-shot deployment: the policy trained purely on NeRF-rendered images is flown on a physical quadrotor with no fine-tuning.

Headline results reported in the abstract; per-track numbers and baseline comparisons await the full-PDF pass.
MeasureReported value
Success rate95.8% over 30 live hardware flights
Average gate error~10 cm, through 38 cm-radius gates
Gate crossings~120 in total
Track shapes3 — circle, U-turn, figure-8
Real-world training datanone — zero-shot, no fine-tuning

A ~10 cm average error through a 38 cm-radius gate leaves a comfortable margin, and the error is achieved without any external motion capture at deployment — pose comes from the onboard NPE-plus-Kalman stack. Three distinct track shapes suggest the policy is not memorising a single trajectory, though generalisation to unseen tracks is a separate question (§5).

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors: none listed in the abstract; the limitations section awaits the full-PDF extraction pass.
  • Our open questions (marked as ours, to be checked against the full text): how much capture effort — photographs, poses, fitting time — does a NeRF of each new environment cost, and does that effort undercut the "unlimited data" advantage? How robust is the policy to lighting that differs from the captured scene, since the radiance field freezes one illumination condition into the training distribution? And does the recipe generalise beyond gate-relative navigation, where the task conveniently supplies a well-defined visual anchor for the NPE?

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

FalconGym is the perception-side complement to the dynamics-side transfer papers in this list, and every ingredient is within this project's reach. NeRF capture and training are feasible on the project's RTX A4000; 38 cm-class gates are printable on the Bambu H2C, so the physical benchmark can be replicated exactly. The NPE-plus-Kalman stack is a direct candidate perception pipeline for the Holybro X500 with the Jetson Orin Nano handling inference. Methodologically, the imitation-in-sim route sidesteps reward design entirely — the hardest part of an RL formulation for navigation — which makes it the lowest-risk starting point for the pilot experiment.
