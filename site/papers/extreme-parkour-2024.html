Extreme parkour with legged robots

Xuxin Cheng, Kexin Shi, Ananye Agarwal & Deepak Pathak

TL;DR A single neural-net policy, trained end-to-end in simulation with large-scale RL and seeing only a front-facing depth camera, makes a low-cost Unitree A1 quadruped do extreme parkour: a high jump onto a 0.5 m box (twice its hip height), a long jump across a 0.8 m gap (twice its foot separation), handstands, and runs across tilted ramps. The recipe is a two-phase teacher–student distillation — a privileged "scandots" policy trained with inner-product rewards and regularised online adaptation, distilled via DAgger into a convnet-GRU depth policy — trained on a single RTX 3090 in under 20 hours and run onboard a Jetson NX at 50 Hz.

1 · The problem

Parkour asks a robot for exactly what cheap hardware is worst at: precisely timed, dynamic whole-body motion, launched from what a noisy camera says about an obstacle. The platform here is deliberately modest — a Unitree A1 whose thigh joint stands 26 cm off the ground with a 40 cm body, a RealSense depth camera that delivers frames at an unsteady 10±2 Hz, and what the paper calls "imprecise sensing and actuation".

The authors' claim is that none of this has to be fixed in hardware: "a single neural net policy operating directly from a camera image, trained in simulation with large-scale RL, can overcome imprecise sensing and actuation to output highly precise control behavior end-to-end" (abstract). The interesting question for this reading list is how a simulation-only training pipeline absorbs those imprecisions — and how much of that machinery is platform-independent.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Phase 1 — RL from privileged scandots

The first policy is trained with model-free RL in simulation with access to information the real robot will never have. It "takes as input, the proprioception x, scandots m, target heading d̂, walking flag W and commanded speed vcmd" (Sec 3.2). Terrain difficulty is handled by an automatic curriculum: "robots are first initialized in easy levels. They are promoted to harder ones if they traverse more than half the length, and demoted to an easier one if they travel less than half the expected distance" (Sec 3.2). Alongside the policy, "regularized online adaptation (ROA)" trains "an adaptation module to estimate environment properties" from observation history.

Concept · scandots as a training-time sensor abstraction (ours)

"Scandots" — in this literature, a grid of terrain-height samples queried around the robot — are a perfect sensor that only exists in simulation: noiseless, dense, and independent of camera pose. Training the first policy on scandots decouples learning what to do about terrain from learning to see terrain, and leaves a clean supervision target for the later vision policy. ROA sits in the same lineage as RMA's latent adaptation: a module that recovers unobserved environment properties from how recent observations respond to recent actions.

3.2 Reward design by inner products

The paper's stated contribution here is "a simple yet effective inner-product reward design principle for general robot base motion" (introduction). Velocity tracking rewards the projection of the body velocity onto the target direction, capped at the commanded speed (the paper's Eq. 2):

$$ r_{tracking} = \min\!\left(\langle \mathbf{v},\, \hat{\mathbf{d}}_w \rangle,\; v^{cmd}\right) $$
The paper's Eq. (2), Sec 3.1. Our gloss: \(\mathbf{v}\) is the body velocity, \(\hat{\mathbf{d}}_w\) the target direction, \(v^{cmd}\) the commanded speed.

Foot placement near terrain edges is penalised (Eq. 3), and a "stylized" reward gates the handstand behaviour on a binary switch (Eq. 4):

$$ r_{clearance} = -\sum_{i=0}^{4} c_i \cdot M[p_i], \qquad r_{stylized} = W \cdot \left[0.5 \cdot \langle \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{fwd},\, \hat{\mathbf{c}} \rangle + 0.5\right]^2 $$
The paper's Eqs. (3) and (4), Sec 3.1. Our gloss: \(c_i=1\) if foot \(i\) contacts the ground and \(M[p_i]=1\) iff point \(p_i\) lies within 5 cm of a terrain edge; \(\hat{\mathbf{v}}_{fwd}\) is the robot's forward unit vector, \(\hat{\mathbf{c}}\) the desired-direction unit vector, and \(W\) a binary on/off switch.
Concept · why inner-product rewards travel well (ours)

An inner-product tracking term has almost nothing platform-specific in it: it never references legs, gaits or joint targets, only "move the base along this direction, up to this speed". That is why the same scalar can drive a jump, a ramp run and a handstand once the direction and the \(W\) switch change — and why it is a candidate template for base-motion rewards on entirely different vehicle classes.

3.3 Phase 2 — distilling to a deployable depth policy

The phase-1 policy consumes scandots and a ground-truth heading, neither of which exists on the real robot, so "we use supervised learning to obtain a deployable policy which automatically estimates these quantities" (Sec 3.3). Concretely, "we replace the scandots input to the base policy with a convnet-GRU pipeline that accepts depth", and the network "is trained using DAgger, with ground truth actions from the phase 1 policy". The target direction is recovered with a mixture-of-teacher-student (MTS) scheme; the paper's switching rule is obs_θ = {θ_pred if |θ_pred − d̂_w| < 0.6; d̂_w otherwise} — the student's own predicted direction is used only while it stays close to the ground truth, which prevents its errors from dragging training off-distribution.

Concept · why the depth branch needs a GRU (ours)

The camera delivers frames at roughly 10 Hz while the policy acts at 50 Hz, and any single depth image is noisy and partially occluded by the robot's own pitch. A recurrent encoder lets the policy integrate obstacle geometry over time and keep acting on its memory of the terrain between frames — the depth-side analogue of what ROA's history-based module does for dynamics.

3.4 The deployment stack

On the robot, "the Intel RealSense D435 inside the head of the robot … captures images at 10±2 Hz"; images are downsampled to 58×87. "We run both depth backbone (10Hz) and the base policy (50Hz) on the Jetson NX and communicate via UDP" (Sec 4.1). The timing trick worth remembering: the pipeline "enforce[s] constant depth latency of 0.08s", so simulation trains against the same fixed sensor delay the real loop will exhibit — latency is treated as a modelled property, not a nuisance. Two honesty notes from the S4 pass: the fetched text never names the simulator, and the RL algorithm is described only as model-free RL (PPO is cited in the bibliography but never stated as the algorithm used), so neither is asserted here.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): the material here is genuinely thin — the fetched text has no dedicated Limitations or Conclusion section. The only forward-looking statement is one sentence in the brief Discussion (Sec 5), which we paraphrase: the robot is an expert at moving around the world but should also be able to manipulate objects, and a promising research direction is extending the same basic approach to mobile manipulators. No explicit failure-case or quantitative-limitation analysis appears in the fetched pages.
  • Our open questions (ours): with no stated simulator, no named RL algorithm and no domain-randomisation table in the fetched text, how reproducible is the sim-to-real recipe from the paper alone? How far does the fixed 0.08 s latency assumption hold when the camera itself varies at 10±2 Hz? And where is the failure envelope — the text scales the feats against robot geometry (2× hip height, 2× foot separation) but reports no success rates at those extremes.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

This is a quadruped paper, and the platform mismatch is real: nothing here about contact scheduling, foot clearance or handstands maps onto an X500 V2 airframe. Our judgement is that four mechanisms nonetheless transfer almost directly. First, the inner-product reward principle (\(\min(\langle\mathbf{v},\hat{\mathbf{d}}\rangle, v^{cmd})\)) is a clean, tuning-light template for UAV velocity- and heading-command tracking. Second, the two-phase distillation — privileged sim policy → onboard depth policy via DAgger — is the same recipe one would use to distil a privileged simulated UAV policy into an Orin-Nano vision policy. Third, enforcing a constant sensor latency in simulation (0.08 s here, to match a ~10 Hz RealSense) is exactly the timing trick a depth camera + Orin Nano loop needs. Fourth, the whole system trains in simulation on one RTX 3090 in under 20 hours and runs at 50 Hz on a Jetson-class device — a concrete compute-and-latency budget analogue for our deployment target. The caveat cuts the other way: with the simulator unnamed, no randomisation table and no limitations section, the robustness recipe must be inferred, not copied.
