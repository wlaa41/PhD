ANYmal Parkour: agile navigation for quadrupedal robots

David Hoeller, Nikita Rudin, Dhionis Sako & Marco Hutter

ETH-RSL per corpus manifest · arXiv 2306.14874 (no journal reference stated on arXiv; Sci. Robotics 2024 per corpus manifest, to confirm) · arXiv:2306.14874 zero-shot* triage score 88 = 20+25+20+15+8 S4 · S5 pending

TL;DR A 55 kg ANYmal D quadruped that chains parkour obstacles on its own: a perception network on an onboard Jetson Orin rebuilds the terrain from a LiDAR and six depth cameras, a 5 Hz navigation policy picks one of five frozen locomotion skills — walk, jump, climb up, climb down, crouch — and feeds it commands, and the whole stack is trained with PPO in Isaac Gym (4,096 parallel agents) "without expert demonstration, offline computation, a priori knowledge of the environment". On real hardware it crosses consecutive 1 m boxes and gaps at up to 2 m/s, beating hard-coded trajectories in the paper's three-terrain comparison (98.2/96.3/97.6% vs 95.3/60.9/75.3% — Table I values as recorded at S4; the surrounding sentence was not captured verbatim). Wording note: the paper never uses the literal term "zero-shot" — the badge is our classification (see the labelling note in §4).

1 · The problem

Parkour asks a legged robot to do three hard things at once: perceive obstacles from noisy onboard sensors, decide in real time which dynamic manoeuvre to attempt next, and then execute that manoeuvre near the limits of the hardware. The paper's claim is that this entire stack can be learned, and that the result "can plan a path for challenging scenarios without expert demonstration, offline computation, a priori knowledge of the environment".

The difficulty the paper sets up is one of integration rather than of any single skill. Climbing a 1 m box, jumping a 1 m gap and squeezing under a 0.4 m passage are distinct behaviours with distinct failure modes, and a planner that sequences them has to make a long chain of correct decisions where a single wrong skill choice ends the run — the authors report that this makes convergence genuinely hard, needing many iterations and a hand-designed curriculum. The wager of the paper is that a carefully staged, modular training pipeline can get there anyway, entirely in simulation, with domain randomisation as the only bridge to the real robot.

2 · The approach at a glance

The system is a three-module hierarchy trained back-to-front. First, five low-level locomotion skills are trained with PPO on randomised obstacle fields, reading a noise-perturbed 2.5D elevation map; they are then frozen. Next, a perception network learns to reconstruct terrain occupancy — and a latent belief vector — from LiDAR and depth-camera point clouds. Finally, a navigation policy learns, on top of the frozen skills, to pick which skill to fire and what local command to give it.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Three modules, eight networks

The architecture separates perception, navigation and locomotion: "the navigation module guides the locomotion module in the environment by selecting which skill to activate and providing intermediate commands". The two control layers run at very different rates — "the navigation policy runs at 5 Hz while the locomotion module operates at 50 Hz" — and the perception module runs alongside them on a dedicated NVIDIA Jetson Orin. In total the pipeline comprises eight neural networks, a figure the authors themselves flag as a training burden (see §5).

Concept · why a hierarchy at all (ours)

A single end-to-end policy would have to discover jumping, climbing and route choice inside one network, with one reward. Splitting the problem means each locomotion skill can be trained to specialist quality on its own dense reward, and the navigation layer then faces a far smaller problem: a discrete choice among five competent behaviours plus a low-dimensional command. Freezing the skills keeps the navigation layer's world stationary while it learns — the same manager–worker logic as classic hierarchical RL, applied at the perception/planning/actuation seams.

3.2 Locomotion: five specialist skills on a noisy elevation map

Five skills — walk, jump, climb up, climb down, crouch — are trained with PPO in "the Isaac Gym simulator ... 4096 agents in parallel" on obstacle courses where "the dimensions of the individual obstacles and the target's and robot's initial position are randomized". Once trained, "the locomotion policies of the inner loop are frozen throughout training" of the navigation layer. Their terrain input is "a 2.5D elevation map around the robot, which can directly be computed from the point cloud output of the perception module", and robustness comes from corrupting that map during training — "adding noise to individual points and shifting the map up to 7.5 cm in all directions".

$$ \mathbb{1}_{t^*\lt 1}\left(1 - 0.5\,\lVert \mathbf{r}_{xy} - \mathbf{r}^*_{xy}\rVert\right) $$
The locomotion position-tracking reward as given in the paper's Table S2, weighted 10: it rewards reaching the commanded planar position \(\mathbf{r}^*_{xy}\) within the allotted time. The prose gloss is ours.
Concept · the elevation map as a sensor contract (ours)

Training the skills on an elevation map rather than on raw sensor streams fixes an interface between perception and control: the perception stack's whole job becomes "produce this map from real sensors", and the noise injected at training time — point noise plus shifts of up to 7.5 cm — defines exactly how imperfect that map is allowed to be before the skills degrade. This is the training-time sensor-abstraction pattern this corpus keeps meeting: simulate the representation, not the sensor.

3.3 Perception: reconstructing the world it cannot fully see

The perception module turns LiDAR and depth-camera point clouds into terrain occupancy, obstacle centroids and a latent belief vector for the planner. It uses "a multi-resolution scheme that consists of two networks operating at different scales", with memory built in auto-regressively: "the previous output is transformed into the current frame and concatenated with the measurement". Robustness again comes from corruption at training time — the "data augmentation procedure consists of perturbing the position of the points, adding random blobs, removing patches of points, and noisifying the robot's position". At deployment, "the perception module is implemented on an NVIDIA Jetson Orin".

3.4 Navigation: a hybrid-action PPO planner with a distance curriculum

The navigation policy's action space mixes a discrete decision (which skill) with continuous ones (where to send it, with what heading, in how much time). To handle this, "we modify the PPO algorithm and augment the actor's multilayer perceptron with a hybrid output ... a Gaussian distribution for the commands and a categorical distribution for skill activation". Convergence is helped along by a curriculum: "we employ a curriculum where we first place the global targets close to the robots' starting positions and then move them further away".

$$ \mathbb{1}_{t^*=0}\left(40\,\mathbb{S}_N - \lVert \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}^*_G\rVert\right) $$
The navigation position-tracking reward as given in the paper's Table S3, weighted 0.15: a sparse terminal reward for reaching the global goal \(\mathbf{r}^*_G\). Our S4 record does not capture the paper's definition of \(\mathbb{S}_N\), so we leave that symbol unglossed rather than guess.
Concept · hybrid action heads (ours)

Standard PPO implementations assume one distribution family over the whole action vector — usually a diagonal Gaussian. A skill-selecting planner breaks that assumption: "which skill" is categorical, "where and when" is continuous. Factorising the actor's output into a Gaussian head and a categorical head lets one network learn both at once — a small change to the algorithm with a large change in what the policy can express.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): scalability to more diverse scenarios is untested — the demonstrations cover a small number of distinct environmental modules, and settings such as collapsed buildings would demand far broader obstacle variety ("The scalability of the method to more diverse scenarios remains to be tested"); training is time-consuming and brittle to change, since the pipeline uses eight neural networks each needing separate tuning, and modifying one module often forces retraining others; navigation convergence is hard, requiring many iterations and a hand-designed curriculum because the policy must make a long sequence of correct decisions with many failure paths; the perception network can hallucinate a stair behind climbing boxes in sensor blind spots, and tables are sometimes inflated when the robot crawls underneath (errors self-correct with better visibility); as future work, the authors suggest joint training of all components and pre-training the navigation module with expert demonstrations.
  • Our open questions (ours): would the 5 Hz planner / 50 Hz skills split survive aerial dynamics, where a wrong skill choice cannot be absorbed by standing still? How much of the eight-network training burden is intrinsic to the modular design, and how much could a smaller skill library shed? And what fraction of the Jetson Orin perception load would fit on the smaller Orin Nano this project actually carries?

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Platform mismatch, stated plainly: this is a 55 kg legged robot, not a UAV, and nothing in the paper touches aerial dynamics. It earns its place as a pipeline template. Three things transfer. First, a compute proof point: the heavy learned perception module runs in real time on a Jetson Orin onboard — a concrete calibration for what our smaller Orin Nano can and cannot host. Second, the hierarchy is a clean recipe for decoupling a slow, learned navigation layer (5 Hz, picking among frozen skills) from fast reactive control (50 Hz), portable to a UAV obstacle course on the X500 V2. Third, the sim-to-real bridge is exactly the zero-shot pattern this project is committed to: everything trained in Isaac Gym, elevation-map noise plus geometry randomisation, no hardware fine-tuning. The caveats cut the other way — LiDAR plus six depth cameras and an eight-network training pipeline are well beyond an X500/RoboMaster EP budget, so what we would borrow is the shape of the recipe, not its ingredient list.
