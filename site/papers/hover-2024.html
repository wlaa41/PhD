HOVER: one whole-body controller for many humanoid modes

Tairan He, Wenli Xiao, Toru Lin, Zhengyi Luo, Zhenjia Xu, Zhenyu Jiang, Jan Kautz, Changliu Liu, Guanya Shi, Xiaolong Wang, Linxi Fan & Yuke Zhu

TL;DR One network replaces a shelf of specialist controllers. HOVER is a single whole-body policy for a 19-DOF Unitree H1 humanoid that supports over 15 control modes — spanning navigation, loco-manipulation and tabletop manipulation — where prior work trained one controller per command interface. The unifying idea: full-body kinematic motion imitation is a shared abstraction across all of those modes. An oracle policy is trained with goal-conditioned RL (PPO) in IsaacGym on retargeted AMASS human motions, then distilled via DAgger into one multi-mode student, with mode and sparsity command masks sampled from Bernoulli(0.5) so every command combination is practised. Domain randomisation carries the student to the real robot, where it beats the prior specialists on at least 7 of 12 tracking metrics in every mode. (The zero-shot* badge is asterisked — see §4 and §5.)

1 · The problem

A humanoid is asked to speak several control languages at once. Navigation wants root velocity commands, teleoperation wants full-body kinematic poses, tabletop manipulation wants precise joint angles — and the standard practice has been a separately trained specialist controller for each interface.

That fragmentation is expensive twice over: switching task means switching policies, and no specialist benefits from what the others learnt. HOVER's premise is that these interfaces are projections of one underlying skill — tracking whole-body motion — so a single policy trained on that general skill, then taught to accept any partial command, should subsume the specialists. The paper's evidence for the claim is competitive: the one generalist is scored against the prior specialist controllers on their own command modes, in simulation and on a real Unitree H1.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Human motion as the curriculum

Training starts from the large-scale AMASS human motion dataset, retargeted onto the 19-DOF H1 embodiment (Section II-C): "we convert the large-scale human motion dataset [mahmood2019amass] to dataset Q̂ that only contains feasible motions for humanoids." The retargeting filter matters — the oracle is only ever asked to imitate motions the humanoid body can physically perform.

3.2 An oracle teacher with privileged eyes

An oracle whole-body imitation policy is trained with goal-conditioned RL: "We employ the proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithm [schulman2017proximal] to maximize the cumulative discounted reward"

$$ \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=1}^{T} \gamma^{t-1} r_t\right] $$
The paper's objective for the oracle policy (Section II-A). The reward is split into penalty, regularisation and task terms (Sections II-A, II-D).

The oracle observes privileged full-body state — rigid-body position, orientation, linear velocity, angular velocity and the previous action (Section II-D) — quantities a real robot cannot measure directly. That is deliberate: the teacher lives only in IsaacGym and never has to transfer.

Concept · privileged teacher, deployable student (ours)

This is the same asymmetry the corpus keeps meeting (RAPTOR, DTC, HIM): let the teacher cheat with simulator-only state so it becomes genuinely good, then force a student to reproduce the teacher's actions from sensors that exist on hardware. Whatever the student cannot observe must be inferred from what it can — here, a history of its own proprioception.

3.3 One command space, masked into many modes

The command space unifies three families of interface — kinematic position tracking, local joint-angle tracking, and root (velocity/height/orientation) tracking (Section II-B). During distillation the student's goal vector is filtered through two binary masks:

$$ s_t^{g\text{-student}} \triangleq M_{\mathrm{sparsity}} \odot \big[ M_{\mathrm{mode}} \odot s_t^{g\text{-upper}},\; M_{\mathrm{mode}} \odot s_t^{g\text{-lower}} \big] $$
The paper's command-masking equation (Section II-E). "Every bit of the mode and sparsity binary mask is from a Bernoulli distribution ℬ(0.5)" — resampled so the student practises every combination of present and absent commands.
Concept · masking as dropout over commands (ours)

Randomly zeroing parts of the command during training is structurally the same trick as dropout: the policy can never rely on any one command channel being present, so it must learn sensible behaviour for every subset. A mode then stops being a separate controller and becomes just a mask pattern — "track these joints, ignore the rest" — selectable at runtime with no retraining. This is what lets one policy expose 15+ usable modes.

3.4 DAgger distillation into the student

The multi-mode student is produced by distillation: "We perform policy distillation using the DAgger framework [ross2011reduction]" — the student is rolled out in simulation, the oracle is queried for the reference action \(\hat{\mathbf{a}}_t\) at each visited state, and the student is updated to match it:

$$ \mathcal{L} = \lVert \hat{\mathbf{a}}_t - \mathbf{a}_t \rVert_2^2 $$
The paper's action-matching loss (Section II-E). Rolling out under the student's behaviour, DAgger-style, means it learns to correct its own mistakes rather than only the teacher's states.

Crucially, the student's observation is deployment-friendly: joint positions, joint velocities, base angular velocity and the gravity vector stacked over the last 25 timesteps, plus the 25-step action history (Section II-E). No external sensing is stated — proprioception only. The history stack is what compensates for the privileged state the student never sees.

3.5 Crossing to the real robot

The sole stated sim-to-real mechanism is dynamics randomisation, inherited from the group's earlier OmniH2O setup: "We follow the same domain randomization in [he2024omnih2o] to randomize the physical parameters of the simulated environment and humanoids for successful sim-to-real transfer." No on-hardware adaptation, fine-tuning or system identification is described.

4 · Results on real hardware

Hardware is a single platform: "Our real robot employs a 19-DOF Unitree H1 platform with a total mass of around 51.5kg and a height of around 1.8m." Evaluation runs "in both IsaacGym and the real-world Unitree H1 robot", always scoring the one generalist against the prior specialist controllers.

A caveat our label must not hide: the paper never uses the term "zero-shot". What it states is sim-to-real transfer via domain randomisation — train in IsaacGym, deploy on the real H1, with no hardware fine-tuning described. Functionally that is the no-retraining transfer this corpus files under zero-shot, but the words are ours, not the authors' — so the zero-shot* badge carries an asterisk with this wording note, following the fly-in-seconds precedent.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): the controller requires manual mode selection — "Future work will explore further developing automated mode-switching module for real-world applications." That is the only limitation the authors state: the fetched full text has no dedicated limitations section, so the material here is genuinely thin and we record that rather than pad it.
  • Wording note (ours, per the badge asterisk): the paper never claims "zero-shot"; it states sim-to-real transfer via domain randomisation. Our zero-shot tag is a corpus-level judgement about the pipeline (no retraining or identification on hardware is described), not a quotation.
  • Our open questions (ours): training compute, deployment compute, control frequency, firmware and code release are all unstated in the fetched text, so HOVER cannot serve as a latency or compute reference. Beyond the stated future work: how gracefully does the policy behave when the mask pattern switches mid-motion, and how far does the recipe stretch for tasks not expressible as kinematic motion imitation?

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

The headline caveat is platform mismatch: HOVER controls a bipedal humanoid, and nothing here transfers directly to X500 V2 flight dynamics. What earns it a place in the corpus is the methodological core. The multi-mode command-masking plus single-policy distillation recipe is a clean template for collapsing several UAV control interfaces — velocity tracking, waypoint or position control, attitude — into one policy deployable on the Orin Nano, instead of training and swapping separate controllers. The privileged-oracle-to-proprioception-history student distillation via DAgger is exactly the teacher–student pattern this project would use for zero-shot sim-to-real, and the student's proprioception-only, history-stacked observation is the deployment-friendly end of that pattern on constrained onboard compute. Domain randomisation as the sole stated transfer mechanism is the same lever we would pull. But with no stated compute, control-frequency or code-release details, HOVER is a methods and architecture reference, not a hardware or latency reference, for the RoboMaster EP or Orin Nano stack.
