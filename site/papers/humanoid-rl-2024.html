Real-world humanoid locomotion with reinforcement learning

Ilija Radosavovic, Tete Xiao, Bike Zhang, Trevor Darrell, Jitendra Malik & Koushil Sreenath

TL;DR A causal transformer with 1.4M parameters, reading a window of the last 16 observation–action pairs, walks the real Digit humanoid using only joint encoders and an IMU. It is trained entirely in the Isaac Gym simulator — model-free PPO over "an ensemble of randomized environments", with a KL pull towards a state-based teacher that is annealed to zero — and then deployed "to the real world zero-shot", the 50 Hz network commanding a 1 kHz joint PD controller. The policy adapts to terrain and disturbances in-context, "without updating its weights"; over a week of full-day outdoor testing the authors observed no falls.

1 · The problem

A bipedal humanoid is a high-dimensional, contact-rich, permanently-falling machine: every step is a controlled recovery, and the effective dynamics change with each surface, slope and shove. The question this paper poses is whether one learned controller — trained wholly in simulation, sensing nothing but its own joints and an IMU — can walk a real humanoid through ordinary human environments.

Two constraints make the question hard. First, the controller is blind: it receives joint encoder and IMU readings only, so terrain and disturbances must be inferred from how the body responds rather than seen in advance. Second, the transfer is zero-shot — in the authors' words, "we train our model with large-scale model-free reinforcement learning on an ensemble of randomized environments in simulation and deploy it to the real world zero-shot". Nothing is tuned, identified or fine-tuned on the physical robot. What carries the policy across the gap is the claim at the heart of the paper: a history of observation–action pairs contains the evidence "a powerful transformer model can use to adapt its behavior in-context, without updating its weights".

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 A causal transformer over observation–action history

The policy is not a feed-forward map from the current state to an action. It is a causal transformer that consumes a rolling history of proprioceptive observation–action pairs and predicts the next action. The architecture is deliberately compact: "four blocks, each with embedding dimension of 192 and multi-head attention with 4 heads" — "Overall, the model contains 1.4M parameters. We use a context window of 16."

$$ \mathrm{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\tfrac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$
Notation ours — the standard scaled dot-product self-attention that underlies any causal transformer; this exact equation was not confirmed verbatim in the fetched HTML text.
Concept · why a history window is a system identifier

A memoryless policy seeing only the current joint angles cannot tell ice from grass, or a push from a slope — the instantaneous state is ambiguous. A window of (action commanded, motion observed) pairs is not: the discrepancy between what was asked for and what happened is precisely the evidence a classical system identifier would use. Attention over 16 such pairs lets the policy condition its next action on the inferred situation, which is what the authors mean by adapting "in-context, without updating its weights". It is the transformer counterpart of the recurrent-hidden-state trick in RAPTOR and RMA — the same idea, with attention instead of recurrence as the memory mechanism.

3.2 Joint optimisation: PPO plus an annealed teacher

Training is model-free reinforcement learning: "We use the proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithm for training RL policies." But the observation-based policy is not trained alone — the paper adds an imitation term pulling it towards a state-based teacher policy, and optimises the combined loss:

$$ L(\pi_o) = L_{RL}(\pi_o) + \lambda\, D_{KL}(\pi_o \,\|\, \pi_s) $$
The paper's objective: the RL term plus a KL divergence pulling the observation-based policy \(\pi_o\) towards a state-based teacher \(\pi_s\); the weighting factor \(\lambda\) is "gradually annealed to zero over the course of training".
Concept · why anneal the teacher away

Early in training the teacher — which sees privileged simulator state — provides dense guidance, so the student does not have to discover walking from scratch under partial observability. But a teacher trained on privileged state is not the optimum for a blind policy; annealing \(\lambda\) to zero means the final policy is shaped purely by the RL objective under the observations it will actually have on the robot. Scaffold first, then remove the scaffold. (This reading of the annealing's purpose is ours.)

3.3 Randomisation and sensing noise

The "ensemble of randomized environments" varies the "dynamics properties of the robot, control parameters, environment physics" and terrain, and injects "noise and delay to the observations" during training. The policy therefore never trains on one consistent world — it trains on a distribution wide enough that the real Digit, with its real latencies and imperfect sensors, is plausibly one more sample from it.

3.4 The stack, from simulator to robot

Simulation and training: "We use the Isaac Gym simulator (?, ?) to model the rigid-body and contact dynamics of the Digit humanoid robot", and the authors "parallelize training across four A100 GPUs and thousands of randomized environments". Deployment: "We run the neural network policy at 50 Hz and the joint PD controller at 1 kHz", with sensing limited to what the platform exposes — "We can get access to joint encoders and IMU information through the API provided by Agility Robotics." The on-robot compute that hosts the 50 Hz network is not stated in the fetched text, and no code release is stated either.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors (S4, from the arXiv full text, 12 Jul 2026): the learned policy is not perfectly symmetrical — the motors on the two sides of the robot do not produce identical trajectories; the policy does not perfectly track the commanded velocity; and under excessive external disturbances (e.g. a very strong pull on a cable attached to the robot) the robot can still fall.
  • Stated future directions (same source): the approach should be easier to scale with additional data and compute, and to incorporate additional input modalities; the authors believe transformers may facilitate future progress in scaling learning approaches for real-world humanoid locomotion.
  • Our open questions (ours): how much of the final robustness is owed to the context-16 history versus the annealed teacher — the reported ablations cover architecture and context length, not the teacher term; whether contact-driven in-context adaptation carries over to non-contact disturbances such as wind, which is what matters for our platform; and what on-robot compute the 50 Hz policy actually requires, since deployment hardware is not stated.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

The platform mismatch is real and worth stating plainly: this is a bipedal humanoid whose hard problem is contact, not a UAV whose hard problem is aerodynamics, and nothing here validates flight. What transfers is the recipe. A compact causal transformer (1.4M parameters) over a proprioceptive observation–action history, trained with model-free PPO plus domain randomisation and an annealed teacher-KL term, then deployed zero-shot with no fine-tuning — that reads like a template for the X500 V2 pilot experiment. The key transferable idea is adapting "in-context, without updating its weights": a history-conditioned policy that implicitly system-identifies changing dynamics is exactly the robustness a wind-buffeted quadrotor needs. Practically, a 1.4M-parameter transformer at 50 Hz is well within the Jetson Orin Nano's budget, so the architecture is directly reusable. The gap for our setup is that this controller is blind (joint encoders + IMU only) — any vision or RoboMaster-EP-style perception fusion would be net-new work this paper does not address.
