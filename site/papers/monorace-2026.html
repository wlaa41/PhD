MonoRace: Winning Champion-Level Drone Racing with Robust Monocular AI

Stavrow A. Bahnam, Robin Ferede, Till M. Blaha, Anton E. Lang, Erin Lucassen, Quentin Missinne, Aderik E.C. Verraest, Christophe De Wagter & Guido C.H.E. de Croon

TL;DR A racing quadrotor whose only sensors are one monocular rolling-shutter camera and an IMU — every computation onboard, no external tracking of any kind — won the 2025 Abu Dhabi Autonomous Drone Racing Competition (A2RL), beating all competing AI teams and three human world-champion pilots in a direct knockout tournament, at speeds up to 100 km/h. The estimator fuses a gate-segmentation network with a drone dynamics model; a control network bypasses the inner-loop controllers entirely, sending raw motor commands at 500 Hz from the flight controller itself.

1 · The problem

Drone racing is the adversarial benchmark for autonomous flight: an opponent, a clock, and a physical system driven to its limits, with all perception and control squeezed onto a light-weight airframe under hard real-time constraints.

The field's progression has been a steady removal of crutches. Early demonstrations of agile autonomous flight leaned on external motion-capture systems that handed the vehicle a near-perfect state estimate. The state of the art before this paper — as the authors characterize it — was a system with a stereo camera and an IMU that beat human champions, but in a controlled indoor environment. MonoRace removes both crutches at once: no external tracking, and no second camera.

A monocular rolling-shutter camera is arguably the hardest sensible sensor choice for this task. A single camera provides no instantaneous depth, so metric scale must come from somewhere else; and a rolling shutter exposes each image row at a slightly different instant, so at 100 km/h the vehicle's own motion warps the geometry of every frame. It is also the cheapest and lightest choice — which is precisely the point: if champion-level racing survives this sensor budget, the hardware barrier to agile autonomous flight is far lower than the field assumed.

2 · The approach at a glance

Three components carry the system: a robust state estimator that fuses neural-network gate segmentation with a drone dynamics model; an offline optimization that exploits the known geometry of the gates to refine calibration parameters from nothing but logged onboard flight data; and a small guidance-and-control network that skips the classical cascade of inner-loop controllers, writing motor commands directly at 500 Hz. 3 · The method, in detail

3.1 Model-aided monocular state estimation

The measurement model comes from the track itself. Race gates have known geometry and known positions, so once the segmentation network localizes a gate's corners in the image, each detected corner \(x_i\) can be compared against the reprojection of its known 3-D counterpart \(X_i\):

$$ e_i \;=\; x_i \;-\; \pi\!\big( K\, [R \mid t]\, X_i \big), \qquad (R^{*}, t^{*}) \;=\; \arg\min_{R,\,t} \sum_i \lVert e_i \rVert^{2} $$
Notation ours (reconstructed from the abstract's description): \(\pi\) the perspective projection, \(K\) the camera intrinsics, \([R \mid t]\) the camera pose. The paper's exact formulation awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why known track geometry substitutes for depth

A single camera cannot measure depth from one frame — every point along a viewing ray projects to the same pixel. But if the 3-D coordinates of the observed points are already known, the ambiguity collapses: this is the classic perspective-n-point (PnP) problem, and the four corners of a single gate of known size and location are enough to pin down a metric camera pose. The published track map does the job a second camera would otherwise do. The cost is generality — the trick only works where the geometry is known in advance.

3.2 Filtering with a dynamics model

Gate detections arrive at camera rate, and not reliably — a gate may leave the field of view, and the competition environment produced camera interference. The estimator therefore behaves like a model-based filter: a drone dynamics model propagates the state between visual updates, and the segmentation-derived pose corrects it whenever one lands:

$$ \dot{x} = f(x, u), \qquad \hat{x}_k \;=\; \hat{x}_{k \mid k-1} \;+\; K_k \big( z_k - h(\hat{x}_{k \mid k-1}) \big) $$
Notation ours: an EKF-style predict–update cycle, with \(f\) the drone model driven by IMU and command inputs, and \(z_k\) the gate-derived pose measurement. Whether the paper uses an EKF proper or another fusion scheme awaits the full-PDF pass.
Concept · why a drone model bridges the gaps between frames

The control network needs a state estimate at 500 Hz; the camera delivers evidence far less often, and sometimes not at all — interference dropouts, gates out of frame. A dynamics model turns the IMU and the commands already issued into a prediction of where the drone must be now, so the estimate degrades gracefully rather than freezing between detections. The same model helps with the rolling shutter: each image row is exposed at a different instant, and a motion model is exactly what is needed to account for where the camera was as each row was captured.

3.3 Offline calibration refinement from flight logs

The abstract's quietly important contribution: an offline optimization that uses the known gate geometry to refine any state-estimation parameter from onboard flight data alone. After a flight, every logged gate observation is a constraint — the corners must reproject where the camera actually saw them — so calibration parameters \(\theta\) can be re-estimated by minimizing the total reprojection error over the logs:

$$ \theta^{*} \;=\; \arg\min_{\theta} \sum_{\text{flights}} \sum_{i} \big\lVert\, x_i - \pi_{\theta}(X_i) \,\big\rVert^{2} $$
Notation ours: \(\pi_{\theta}\) the projection under calibration parameters \(\theta\). The authors single out the external (extrinsic) camera calibration as the vital parameter this procedure fine-tunes; the full parameterization awaits the full-PDF pass.

No calibration rig, no motion capture — the track is the calibration target. A hard landing that nudges the camera a degree would silently corrupt a fixed calibration; here the next flight's logs repair it. In the method registry this procedure is filed under system-identification rather than as a separate calibration family (merge decision logged in the registry).

3.4 Direct motor-command control at 500 Hz

Guidance and control collapse into a single small network that foregoes the classical inner-loop controllers altogether, mapping its observation directly to the four motor commands, \(a_t = \pi(o_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{4}\) (here \(\pi\) denotes the control policy, distinct from the projection above), executed on the flight controller itself at 500 Hz. Removing the cascade of attitude and rate loops removes their latency and their hand-tuning — but it also removes their safety net, since nothing downstream will clean up a bad network output at racing speeds. That the system nonetheless survived IMU saturation and camera interference in competition is the strongest available evidence for the robustness claim in the title. How the network was trained is not detailed in the abstract and awaits the full-PDF pass.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Flagged by the authors: camera interference and IMU saturation at the venue — presented in the abstract as engineering hurdles the system successfully coped with, not as open failures. The full limitations discussion awaits the full-PDF extraction pass.
  • Ours: the estimator leans on a structured environment — gates of known geometry at known positions. How the approach degrades on tracks without a published map, or in unstructured environments generally, is untested here (and outside the paper's stated scope).
  • Ours: the training regimen for the guidance-and-control network is not described in the abstract, so the simulation-versus-real-data training split — the question this corpus most cares about — is unknown until the full-PDF pass.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

MonoRace is the strongest evidence in the corpus that fully-onboard, monocular, agile autonomous flight is competition-grade in 2025–26 — not a laboratory demonstration but a knockout-tournament win over world-champion humans. Its hardware frugality reframes this project's budget: if a rolling-shutter camera, an IMU and a flight controller suffice for champion-level racing, the project's Jetson Orin Nano is generous rather than limiting. The model-aided estimation line — vision fused with a drone model, refined offline from logs — pairs naturally with the planned PX4 offboard experiments, where the same predict-with-model, correct-with-vision structure applies. And the TU Delft MAVLab lineage (Ferede, De Wagter, de Croon) connects this paper directly to the time-optimal RL line already in the corpus.
