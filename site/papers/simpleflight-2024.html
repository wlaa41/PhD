What Matters in Learning A Zero-Shot Sim-to-Real RL Policy for Quadrotor Control? A Comprehensive Study

TL;DR The field's transfer folklore, put under a controlled lamp. Instead of proposing yet another trick, this Tsinghua study asks which tricks actually matter for zero-shot sim-to-real quadrotor RL, identifies five critical factors, and bundles them into a PPO-based framework called SimpleFlight, trained in the GPU-based OmniDrones simulator. Deployed on a real Crazyflie with no fine-tuning, it cuts trajectory tracking error by more than 50% against state-of-the-art RL baselines — on smooth polynomial trajectories and on infeasible zigzags where the baselines fail. Of the ten papers annotated here, this is the one closest in spirit to this literature review itself: an ablation-driven answer to a question every other page on this site raises implicitly.

1 · The problem

Every zero-shot sim-to-real paper ships a different bundle of transfer tricks — randomisation schemes, observation choices, reward terms, action smoothing — and reports that the bundle worked. Almost none of them report which ingredients carried the result. The recipes accumulate; the evidence for the individual ingredients does not.

The abstract frames the standing tension: classical quadrotor control leans on flat trajectories or time-consuming optimisation, which limits flexibility; RL policies map observations to actions directly, but "often experience instability when deployed in real world". Between those two poles sits an unexamined pile of folklore. Every paper annotated on this site uses a different subset of the registry's methods, and until someone ran controlled comparisons, the field had no way of knowing which subset was load-bearing. Someone had to ablate them. This paper did.

3 · The method, in detail

3.1 The PPO backbone

The abstract states plainly that SimpleFlight is "a PPO-based training framework" — the five factors are integrated around a standard on-policy core, not a bespoke algorithm. That makes the backbone the familiar clipped surrogate:

$$ L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[ \min\!\Big( r_t(\theta)\,\hat{A}_t,\; \operatorname{clip}\!\big(r_t(\theta),\, 1-\epsilon,\, 1+\epsilon\big)\,\hat{A}_t \Big) \right], \qquad r_t(\theta) \;=\; \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t \mid s_t)}. $$
Notation ours — this is the canonical PPO clipped surrogate (Schulman et al., 2017), reconstructed because the abstract only names the algorithm family. The paper's exact objective, and how the five factors modify observations, rewards and training around it, awaits the full-PDF pass.

The contrast with the rest of the corpus is worth recording. Learning to Fly in Seconds was widely read as a PPO result until the S5 full-text pass revealed its trainer is off-policy. With that correction on file, SimpleFlight becomes this corpus's cleanest exemplar of PPO taken all the way to real hardware — the algorithm the field treats as the default, actually validated end-to-end on a physical quadrotor.

3.2 Why an ablation study earns its place here

Concept · the epistemics of "what matters" studies

A literature review and an ablation study are the same experiment run in different media. The review counts how often N papers use a technique and treats frequency as evidence of essentiality; the ablation holds everything else fixed, removes the technique, and measures the damage. The first is cheap and observational, the second expensive and causal. A "what matters" paper converts N papers' worth of folklore into ranked, controlled evidence — which is precisely what this site's method registry tries to approximate by counting. When the two agree, the frequency signal is vindicated; when they disagree, the ablation wins and the registry's weighting should be revisited.

3.3 The five factors — honestly flagged

The five critical factors are the paper's core contribution, and they are not named in the abstract. Under this site's verification law, that means they cannot be listed here yet: no secondary source is trusted to name them, and nothing is invented. The full list is flagged for the S5 full-PDF pass, where the factors will be extracted verbatim and then checked, one by one, against the method registry's frequency counts — the single most direct test this corpus can run of whether counting predicts what controlled ablation confirms.

4 · Results on real hardware

Validation is on a real Crazyflie, and the headline is a relative one: SimpleFlight "achieves more than a 50% reduction in trajectory tracking error compared to state-of-the-art RL baselines". The abstract adds texture on where the margin comes from — the policy "consistently excels across both smooth polynomial trajectories and challenging infeasible zigzag trajectories" on small thrust-to-weight quadrotors, whereas "baseline methods struggle with high-speed or infeasible trajectories". The infeasible-zigzag case is the telling one: a trajectory the platform cannot track exactly punishes brittle policies hardest.

Two reproducibility facts round out the abstract: SimpleFlight is integrated into the GPU-based OmniDrones simulator, and the authors provide open-source access to both the code and the model checkpoints. For a study whose value lies in being checked and reused, that is the right release posture — and it is what makes the reproduction options in section 6 realistic.

5 · Limitations & open questions

  • Stated by the authors: none in the abstract — it contains no limitations statement. Recorded as such rather than paraphrased around; the paper's own limitations section, if one exists, will be quoted after the S5 full-PDF pass.
  • Ours: validation is Crazyflie-only. A palm-sized platform with a small thrust-to-weight ratio is the gentlest possible hardware test, and the same scale question already raised for Learning to Fly in Seconds applies here: do the five factors keep their ranking on heavier, faster airframes?
  • Ours: the five factors were identified and ablated inside one simulator. Whether their ranking is a property of quadrotor sim-to-real transfer in general, or partly a property of OmniDrones, is exactly the kind of question a second simulator — an Isaac-based or differentiable-physics stack — would answer.

6 · Relevance to this PhD

Why this paper matters here

Potentially the highest-leverage paper in the corpus for this project. First, if the five factors turn out to overlap with the method registry's high-count entries, the registry's frequency analysis gains independent experimental confirmation — an ablation study agreeing with a literature count is the strongest validation this review can get. Second, OmniDrones (Tsinghua, GPU-based) is a concrete candidate for the second simulator in sim-to-sim validation — the E2E-Fly pipeline stage this project adopted as I2.4. Third, open code and open checkpoints on a Crazyflie make SimpleFlight a possible secondary reproduction target alongside the designated Learning to Fly in Seconds baseline: same airframe, opposite algorithm family.
